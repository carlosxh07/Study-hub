EU Law · Session V
Wrap-Up
BEPS & global tax reform, legal systems, rule of law, and EU history overview.
BEPS & Global Tax Reform

The BEPS (Base Erosion & Profit Shifting) project was triggered by public outrage over legal but aggressive tax avoidance by multinationals (especially GAFAs). Led by OECD/G20, involving 148+ countries.

  • Why it matters: USD 100–240bn lost globally to aggressive tax planning annually — 4–10% of global CIT revenue. Developing countries suffer most (high reliance on CIT, low enforcement capacity).
  • Pillar 1 — Reallocates taxing rights to market jurisdictions (where consumers are, not where HQ is). Targets MNEs with >€20bn revenue & >10% profitability. Currently stalled — USA withdrew support under Trump. Result: unilateral digital service taxes emerging globally (patchwork).
  • Pillar 2 — 15% global minimum corporate tax. Applies to MNE groups with >€750m annual revenue (~8,000 groups worldwide). Implemented in EU via Directive 2022/2523 (in force 31.12.2023).
  • Pillar 2 key rules: IIR (Income Inclusion Rule — top-up at parent level), UTPR (Undertaxed Payments Rule — backstop), QDMTT (domestic top-up tax — jurisdictions apply top-up to themselves first).
  • EU implementation: BEPS measures implemented via ATAD (Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive). Peer reviews monitor implementation of BEPS minimum standards.
  • Tax terminology distinctions: Tax saving (legal, no avoidance intent) → Tax planning (legal, structuring affairs) → Tax avoidance (legal but contrary to legislative intent) → Aggressive tax planning → Tax evasion (illegal).
Legal Systems & Rule of Law
  • Forms of legal systems: common law (case-based, UK/USA), code law (civil law, continental Europe), religious law, customary law.
  • Rule of law (Art. 2 TEU) — 6 aspects: (1) legality, (2) legal certainty, (3) prohibition of arbitrary executive power, (4) effective judicial protection, (5) separation of powers, (6) equality before the law.
  • Council of Europe (≠ EU!) — 46 member states, promotes rule of law, human rights, democracy. Home of European Court of Human Rights (ECHR). Russia excluded 2022. Not to be confused with: European Council, Council of the EU, or International Criminal Court (independent UN body).
  • German Basic Law (GG) — 5 state principles: Democracy (Art. 20(2) GG), Rechtsstaat, Republic, Federalism (Bund + 16 Länder), Sozialstaat. Safeguarded by Federal Constitutional Court (FCC) in Karlsruhe.
  • Separation of powers: Horizontal (legislative/executive/judicial — Montesquieu 1748, earlier: Locke 1689, Aristotle 4th c. BC) and Vertical (federal/Länder).
  • Democracy distinction: Popular sovereignty = source of power (people). Democracy = manner of exercising power. Direct vs indirect (referenda in Germany generally not possible — Art. 20 GG).
EU History — Key Timeline
  • 1945: WWII ends. UN founded (June 26). Churchill calls for "United States of Europe" (Sept 19, 1946) — but without UK.
  • 1949: Council of Europe founded (May 5).
  • 1950: Schuman Plan (May 9 — now Europe Day).
  • 1951: Treaty of Paris → ECSC (European Coal & Steel Community). 1957: Treaties of Rome → EEC + Euratom. 1965: Merger Treaty (institutions unified).
  • 1979: First direct EP elections. 1985: Schengen Agreement. 1987: Single European Act (extended competences, strengthened EP).
  • 1992: Maastricht Treaty (monetary union). 1993: Copenhagen Criteria (political, economic, acquis conditions for accession).
  • 2002: Euro introduced. 2004: Big eastward enlargement (10 CEE + Malta + Cyprus).
  • 2009: Lisbon Treaty → Charter of Fundamental Rights becomes legally binding.
  • 2016: Brexit vote. 2022: Russian aggression on Ukraine.
  • Charlemagne (747–814) — "Father of Europe": coronation 800 AD by Pope Leo III, Christianisation, administrative/educational reforms. Charlemagne Prize of Aachen awarded in his memory.

EU Law · Session VI
Nature of EU Law
The 5 landmark cases + interactive case analysis tool. Focus on the method.
The 5 Landmark Cases — Overview

These cases define how EU law relates to national law. The professor's focus: not memorising facts, but mastering the method — how to identify a legal issue and apply the right principle.

Exam method: (1) Identify conflict (EU vs. national law) → (2) Apply primacy (Costa) → (3) Check direct effect (Van Gend) → (4) National court duty (Simmenthal) → (5) Directive not implemented? → State liability (Francovich).
EU Legal Order — Full Structure
  • Primary law (Art. 1(3) TEU): TEU + TFEU + Charter of Fundamental Rights — all have equal legal value since Lisbon 2009. Art. 6(1) TEU makes Charter binding.
  • Regulation (Art. 288(2) TFEU): Binding in entirety, directly applicable in all MS — no national implementation needed. Example: passenger rights regulation.
  • Directive (Art. 288(3) TFEU): Binding as to the result to be achieved. MS chooses form and method. Can have vertical direct effect once implementation deadline lapses + provision is clear, precise, unconditional. No horizontal direct effect (individual vs. individual).
  • Decision (Art. 288(4) TFEU): Binding in entirety, but only on specific addressees (MS or individuals).
  • Recommendations & Opinions: Not binding — soft law. Used for guidance.
The 5 Cases — Quick Reference
  • Van Gend en Loos (1963) — Direct Effect. Individuals can rely on EU law directly. Requirements: clear, precise, unconditional. Art. 30 TFEU (ex Art. 12 EEC).
  • Costa v. ENEL (1964) — Primacy. EU law prevails over all national law including later legislation. MS permanently limited sovereignty. Without primacy, EU law would be meaningless.
  • Internationale Handelsgesellschaft (1970) — Primacy extends to constitutional law. National constitutions cannot override EU law. But: fundamental rights exist as general principles of EU law (pre-Charter).
  • Simmenthal (1978) — Immediate effectiveness. National courts must immediately disapply conflicting national law — no waiting for constitutional court or legislature. MS cannot enact new conflicting laws.
  • Francovich (1991) — State liability. MS must compensate individuals for damage from failure to implement EU law. 3 conditions: directive grants rights, content identifiable, causal link. + Deadline elapsed.
Direct Effect — Types & Limits
  • Vertical direct effect: individual relies on EU law against the state → allowed for both Treaty provisions and directives (once deadline lapses).
  • Horizontal direct effect: individual relies on EU law against another private party → allowed for Treaty provisions, but NOT for directives.
  • Why no horizontal DE for directives? Directives are addressed to MS — they only bind MS. Imposing obligations on individuals would bypass national implementation.
  • Remedy when no DE: Francovich state liability. Individual sues the MS for failing to implement, not the private party.
  • Indirect effect (consistent interpretation): National courts must interpret national law in light of the directive's purpose — even if direct effect not available.
1963
Van Gend en Loos · Case 26/62
⚡ Direct Effect
+ Show details
Facts: Dutch company (Van Gend & Loos) imported urea-formaldehyde from Germany. Dutch customs reclassified it under a higher-duty category, raising the duty from 3% to 8% — violating the EEC standstill clause on customs duties.

Legal question: Can individuals directly invoke EU law in national courts?

ECJ holding: Yes. The EEC constitutes a "new legal order of international law" — unlike ordinary international law, it creates rights for individuals which national courts must protect. The standstill provision (now Art. 30 TFEU) had direct effect.

3 Requirements for direct effect: Provision must be (1) clear, (2) precise, (3) unconditional — creating a right without further legislative action.

Significance: First time EU law was declared to create enforceable individual rights. Foundation of the entire EU legal order as we know it.
1964
Costa v. ENEL · Case 6/64
⚡ Primacy of EU Law
+ Show details
Facts: Italy nationalised its electricity sector, creating Enel. Costa (advised by Prof. Stendardi) refused to pay his Enel bill, arguing the nationalisation law violated EEC law. The Italian Constitutional Court had already ruled that the later Italian law should prevail over earlier EEC law.

ECJ holding: EU law is an integral part of national legal systems and takes precedence. MS permanently limited their sovereign rights by joining. A later national law cannot override earlier EU law.

Reasoning: If MS could override EU law by passing national legislation, the EU's functioning would be put in jeopardy. The EEC has a "special and original nature" that distinguishes it from general international law.

Consequence: National courts must apply EU law over conflicting national law — regardless of when the national law was passed.
1970
Internationale Handelsgesellschaft
⚡ Primacy over Constitutional Law
+ Show details
Facts: German private company faced forfeiture of its export licence deposit under an EU regulation. German court argued this violated German constitutional fundamental rights (Art. 2(1) — general freedom of action, Art. 14 GG — property rights).

ECJ holding (double principle):
(1) EU law cannot be reviewed against national constitutional law. The yardstick for EU law's validity is EU law only. Only the ECJ can declare EU law invalid — national courts cannot.
(2) BUT: fundamental rights form an integral part of the general principles of EU law, drawn from the constitutional traditions of MS and international instruments (esp. ECHR).

Historical context: At the time (1970), there was no written EU catalogue of fundamental rights. This case created the foundation for EU fundamental rights protection.

Legacy: Led eventually to the Charter of Fundamental Rights (2000, binding since Lisbon 2009).

Tension: German Federal Constitutional Court ("Solange" judgments) initially resisted this — still an ongoing constitutional dialogue.
1978
Simmenthal · Case 106/77
⚡ Immediate Effectiveness
+ Show details
Facts: Italian authorities required Simmenthal to pay fees for veterinary/public health inspections at the Italian border — incompatible with EU free movement of goods rules. Italy argued its national law (enacted under EC Treaty) would take precedence.

ECJ holding:
(1) Every national court must apply EU law in its entirety and protect rights conferred on individuals.
(2) Any conflicting national provision — even those enacted AFTER the EU rule — must be rendered inapplicable immediately.
(3) No need to wait for a constitutional court ruling or parliamentary repeal. National courts act immediately.
(4) MS also cannot enact new laws that conflict with EU law.

Why it matters: Operationalises primacy. Costa said EU law prevails in theory; Simmenthal tells courts exactly what to do about it in practice.

Exam trap: Courts do NOT need to refer to a constitutional court, legislative body, or the ECJ before disapplying national law.
1991
Francovich & Bonifaci · C-6/90, C-9/90
⚡ State Liability
+ Show details
Facts: Directive 80/987 required MS to create a guarantee fund protecting employees' wages if their employer went insolvent. Italy failed to implement this directive by the 1983 deadline. Francovich and Bonifaci were owed wages by insolvent employers and had no remedy because the fund didn't exist.

ECJ holding: MS are liable to compensate individuals for damage caused by their failure to implement EU law. This flows from the full effectiveness of EU law — if directives could be ignored without consequence, they would lose their binding force.

3 Conditions:
(1) The directive must grant rights to individuals.
(2) The content of those rights must be identifiable from the directive's text.
(3) There must be a causal link between the MS's breach and the individual's damage.
+ The implementation deadline must have elapsed.

Vertical DE vs Francovich: If a directive has direct effect → individual can rely on it against the state directly. If no direct effect (content not sufficiently clear, or horizontal situation) → Francovich state liability applies instead.

Why it matters: Without liability, MS would have no incentive to implement directives promptly. Completes the EU enforcement picture.

Art. 1
TEU — Foundation
EU founded on TEU + TFEU. Equal legal value.
The EU is founded on the TEU and TFEU, which have the same legal value. The EU replaces and succeeds the European Community. Key: the two treaties together are the constitutional basis of the EU.
Art. 2
TEU — Values & Rule of Law
Founding values incl. rule of law, human dignity, democracy.
6 aspects of rule of law: (1) legality, (2) legal certainty, (3) prohibition of arbitrary executive power, (4) effective judicial protection, (5) separation of powers, (6) equality before the law. Violation → Art. 7 TEU procedure (infringement).
Art. 5
TEU — Conferral
EU acts only within limits of conferred competences.
The EU has no inherent powers — all competence flows from explicit conferral in the Treaties. Powers not conferred remain with Member States (cf. subsidiarity and proportionality also in Art. 5 TEU). This is the fundamental limit on EU authority.
Art. 6(1)
TEU — Charter Binding
Charter has same legal value as Treaties since Lisbon 2009.
Since the Lisbon Treaty (2009), the Charter of Fundamental Rights is legally binding primary law with the same status as TEU and TFEU. Before Lisbon: fundamental rights existed only as general principles of EU law (Internationale Handelsgesellschaft, 1970).
Art. 19
TEU — Role of ECJ
Court ensures EU law observed. One judge per MS.
Establishes the CJEU (ECJ + General Court + specialised courts). One judge per Member State. Judges must be independent — politically, financially, mentally. Court ensures uniform interpretation and application of EU law across all MS.
Art. 252
TFEU — Advocate General
AGs deliver independent, impartial opinions to assist the Court.
Advocates General deliver reasoned, impartial opinions — not binding but highly influential. Currently 11 AGs. Role: assist in achieving open, consistent application of EU law. Their opinions often shape future case law even if not followed immediately.
Art. 267
TFEU — Preliminary Ruling
National courts refer EU law questions to ECJ for uniform interpretation.
Primary mechanism for uniform application of EU law. Lower courts may refer; courts of last instance must refer (obligation). ECJ answers on: (1) interpretation of EU law, or (2) validity of EU acts (only ECJ can declare EU law invalid — Internationale Handelsgesellschaft). Van Gend and Costa both came through this procedure.
Art. 288
TFEU — Secondary Law
Defines all types of EU legal acts and their binding nature.
Regulation: Directly applicable — no MS implementation needed. Binding in entirety.
Directive: Binding as to result — MS chooses form & method. Vertical DE possible once deadline lapses.
Decision: Binding on specific addressees only.
Soft law: Recommendations & opinions — not binding but can have legal relevance.
Art. 30
TFEU — No Customs Duties
Prohibits customs duties and charges of equivalent effect between MS.
This was Art. 12 EEC Treaty — the provision at stake in Van Gend en Loos. The ECJ held it had direct effect: it was clear, precise, and unconditional. No MS action needed — Van Gend could rely on it directly. Now codified as Art. 30 TFEU.

Microeconomics · Problem Set 1
Individual Choice
Indifference Curves · MRS · Budget Line · Optimisation · Cobb-Douglas · Leontief · Perfect Substitutes
The Core Problem

Every consumer faces two questions: What can I afford? (budget constraint) and What is best for me? (preferences / utility). The optimal choice is the bundle that maximises utility subject to the budget constraint — graphically, the highest IC that still touches the budget line.

Indifference Curves
  • Definition: All bundles (x₁, x₂) yielding the same utility level k. Set u(x₁,x₂)=k and solve for x₂ as a function of x₁.
  • Key properties: Higher curves = higher utility. Normally convex (prefer averages). Never cross.
  • Example: u = x_A·x_B = 100 → x_B = 100/x_A (hyperbola). At (5,20): slope = −100/25 = −4, so MRS = 4.
  • Weak preference set: All bundles weakly preferred to a given bundle — graphically, the area on and above the IC through that bundle.
Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)
  • Definition: Maximum amount of x₂ willingly given up for one more unit of x₁, keeping utility constant. = Absolute value of IC slope.
  • Formula: MRS = MU₁/MU₂ = (∂u/∂x₁)/(∂u/∂x₂)
  • Cobb-Douglas shortcut: For u = x₁ᵃ·x₂ᵇ: MRS = (a/b)·(x₂/x₁)
  • Why MRS changes: As you get more x₁ and less x₂, x₁ becomes relatively less valuable → MRS falls (diminishing MRS → convex ICs).
The Budget Constraint
  • Formula: p₁x₁ + p₂x₂ = m
  • Slope: −p₁/p₂ = MRT (market exchange rate)
  • Intercepts: x₁-axis: m/p₁. x₂-axis: m/p₂.
  • Tax on good 1: p₁ increases → x₁-intercept (m/p₁) falls → budget line rotates inward around the x₂-intercept (unchanged).
3-Step Solution Method (Standard / Cobb-Douglas)
  • Step 1: Calculate MRS = MU₁/MU₂
  • Step 2: Set MRS = p₁/p₂ → relationship between x₁ and x₂
  • Step 3: Insert into budget line → solve for x₁* and x₂*
  • Why it works: At the optimum, the consumer's subjective willingness to trade (MRS) equals the market's objective rate (p₁/p₂). If MRS > p₁/p₂, buy more x₁.
Budget share rule (Cobb-Douglas): x₁* = [a/(a+b)]·(m/p₁) and x₂* = [b/(a+b)]·(m/p₂). Budget shares are constant regardless of prices.
Special Preference Types
  • Perfect Substitutes u=ax₁+bx₂: MRS=a/b (constant). Compare with p₁/p₂: MRS>p₁/p₂ → buy only x₁; MRS<p₁/p₂ → buy only x₂; MRS=p₁/p₂ → any point on budget line.
  • Leontief u=min{ax₁,bx₂}: L-shaped ICs. Do NOT use MRS=MRT. Set ax₁=bx₂ (kink condition), insert into budget line. Why: no derivative at kink.
Two-Tariff Problems
  • Subtract fixed fee from income to get effective budget
  • Find optimal bundle under each plan using MRS=MRT
  • Compare utility levels → choose higher one
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Using MRS=MRT for Leontief preferences — impossible (no derivative at kink)
  • Forgetting to take absolute value of IC slope for MRS
  • Setting up wrong ratio: MRS = MU₁/MU₂, not MU₂/MU₁
  • For perfect substitutes with MRS=p₁/p₂: the entire budget line is optimal, not just one point
  • Confusing budget shifts (income changes) with rotations (price changes)

Microeconomics · Problem Set 2
Risky Choice
EV · EU · CE · Risk Premium · Insurance as Consumer Choice · MRS = MRT
Risk and Lotteries
  • Lottery: A set of outcomes each with a probability. Σpᵢ=1.
  • EV = Σpᵢ·xᵢ — probability-weighted average of monetary outcomes. No utility involved.
  • EU = Σpᵢ·u(xᵢ) — probability-weighted average of utilities. What rational agents maximise. Apply u(·) first, then weight.
  • Critical distinction: EV and EU are different concepts. A risk-averse person values a lottery at less than its EV (CE < EV).
Risk Attitudes
  • Risk-averse: Concave u(·), u″<0, CE<EV. Example: u=√x.
  • Risk-neutral: Linear u(·), u″=0, CE=EV. Example: u=x.
  • Risk-loving: Convex u(·), u″>0, CE>EV. Example: u=x².
  • Why shape matters: Concave u → each extra euro gives less utility → losing €100 hurts more than gaining €100 helps → consumer dislikes risk.
CE, Risk Premium & Minimum Selling Price
  • CE: u(CE)=EU → solve for CE. Find: compute EU → invert u.
  • Risk Premium: RP=EV−CE. Positive for risk-averse. Maximum premium someone would pay to avoid risk.
  • Minimum selling price: p_min=CE. Set u(p)≥EU → solve. The seller only accepts a certain price ≥ CE.
  • Portia example: EU=1, CE=€1, EV=€4. She would sell for just €1 despite EV being €4.
Key exam trap: "He is risk-averse therefore he rejects the gamble" — WRONG without more information. Risk aversion tells us CE<EV, but not whether CE>0 (accept) or CE<0 (reject). Need exact u(x).
Insurance as a Consumer Choice Problem
  • Reframing: The two "goods" are consumption in the bad state (c_bad) and good state (c_good). Identical to PS1 — just different goods.
  • State-contingent incomes: c_bad = Endow_bad + (1−γ)·x ; c_good = Endow_good − γ·x (premium always paid).
  • Budget line slope: −γ/(1−γ) = MRT. To gain €1 in bad state, give up γ/(1−γ) in good state.
  • MRS: [p·u′(c_bad)] / [(1−p)·u′(c_good)]
  • Fair insurance (γ=p): MRS=MRT → c_bad*=c_good* → full insurance.
  • Unfair insurance (γ>p): Partial insurance only — c_bad*<c_good*.
6-Step Insurance Solution Method
  • 1) Write c_bad and c_good as functions of x
  • 2) Budget line: slope=−γ/(1−γ), use endowment point → intercept
  • 3) Write EU and compute MRS = [p·u′(c_bad)] / [(1−p)·u′(c_good)]
  • 4) Set MRS=MRT → relationship between c_bad* and c_good*
  • 5) Insert into budget line → solve for c_bad* and c_good*
  • 6) Recover x*: use c_bad*=Endow_bad+(1−γ)·x → x*=(c_bad*−Endow_bad)/(1−γ)
Sanity check: Verify MRS=MRT holds for your final c* values. Also: if γ=p, expect c_bad*=c_good*; if γ>p, expect c_bad*<c_good*.
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Saying "he won't take the gamble because he's risk averse" — need the utility function (PS2.2 trap)
  • Forgetting to subtract premium from c_good — premium is always paid
  • Confusing EV and EU — using EV=4 instead of EU=1 for Portia's minimum price
  • Flipping MRS ratio: bad state always in numerator
  • Forgetting the (1−γ) factor when backing out x* from c_bad*

Risky Choice
Lotteries · Expected Utility · Risk Attitudes · Certainty Equivalent · Insurance demand.
Why Risk Matters

Standard consumer theory assumes certainty. In reality — investing, insurance, employment — outcomes depend on circumstances outside your control. The key insight: the same tools from Ch2 (utility functions, budget lines, MRS = MRT) can handle uncertainty with just a reinterpretation.

Modelling Risk: States, Probabilities, Lotteries
  • States of the world: Each state s is a complete description of one scenario (e.g. "river dries up" vs "river flows"). With n states: s₁, s₂, …, sₙ.
  • Probabilities: State i occurs with probability pᵢ, where Σpᵢ = 1. These are objective probabilities.
  • Lottery: L = {p₁,x₁ ; p₂,x₂ ; … ; pₙ,xₙ} — the complete description of any risky situation. Every risky decision is a choice between lotteries.
  • Worked example (lecture): Win €90 (p=0.5) or €0 (p=0.5). u(0)=2, u(90)=12. EV = €45. EU = 0.5×12 + 0.5×2 = 7. Compare to u(45) to determine risk attitude.
Expected Utility Theory
  • Decision rule: EU(L) = Σᵢ pᵢ · u(xᵢ). The consumer chooses the lottery with highest EU.
  • Critical distinction — EV vs EU: EV = Σpᵢxᵢ (average money). EU = Σpᵢu(xᵢ) (average utility after applying u). They are equal only if u is linear.
  • Why u(·) matters: It captures how a person values money — specifically whether marginal utility is decreasing, constant, or increasing. This determines risk attitude entirely.
Risk Attitudes — The Key Chain of Implications
  • Risk-averse: Concave u(·) → decreasing MU → EU(L) < u(EV) → CE < EV → positive risk premium → buys insurance. Intuition: losing €50 hurts more than gaining €50 helps.
  • Risk-neutral: Linear u(·) → constant MU → EU(L) = u(EV) → CE = EV → zero risk premium → indifferent to insurance.
  • Risk-loving: Convex u(·) → increasing MU → EU(L) > u(EV) → CE > EV → negative risk premium → prefers more risk.
Practical significance: Risk aversion is the norm. It is why insurance markets exist — risk-averse people pay a premium to transfer risk to insurers who can pool it.
Certainty Equivalent & Risk Premium
  • Certainty Equivalent (CE): The guaranteed amount making the consumer indifferent to the lottery. Defined by u(CE) = EU(L). Find by: compute EU → invert u.
  • Risk Premium: RP = EV − CE. Maximum the person would pay to swap the lottery for its expected value with certainty. Quantifies how much risk matters.
  • Why it matters practically: RP = maximum insurance premium a person would willingly pay. Any premium below RP is individually rational to accept.
Insurance as State-Contingent Consumption
  • Reframing: Insurance moves consumption between states. The two "goods" are cg (good state consumption) and cb (bad state consumption).
  • Endowment (no insurance): cg = A, cb = A − D. Point (A−D, A) in the (cb, cg) diagram.
  • Coverage K at premium rate γ: cg(K) = A − γK; cb(K) = A − D + (1−γ)K.
  • Budget line slope: −γ/(1−γ) = MRT. To gain €1 in bad state, give up γ/(1−γ) in good state.
Optimal Insurance — MRS = MRT
  • MRS of EU ICs: MRS = [p · u′(cb)] / [(1−p) · u′(cg)]. Don't forget probability weights!
  • Optimality condition: p·u′(cb) / [(1−p)·u′(cg)] = γ/(1−γ)
  • Fair insurance (γ = p): Condition simplifies to u′(cb) = u′(cg) → cb = cg. Risk-averse consumer buys full insurance.
  • Unfair insurance (γ > p): u′(cb) > u′(cg) → cb < cg. Consumer buys partial insurance only. Full insurance line: 45° line where cb = cg.
  • Risk-neutral/loving under unfair insurance: Zero insurance (ICs too flat / concave to benefit).
Exam method: cg(x) and cb(x) → budget line → EU → MRS → MRS=MRT → solve for cb* → recover x* = [cb*−(A−D)]/(1−γ).
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Confusing EV and EU — always apply u(·) before weighting by probability
  • Forgetting probabilities in the MRS — MRS = p·u′(cb)/[(1−p)·u′(cg)], not just u′(cb)/u′(cg)
  • Using p instead of γ for the budget line slope — slope uses γ, not p
  • Assuming full insurance is always optimal — only true when γ = p (fair)
  • Swapping the endowment: bad state = A−D (not A), good state = A (not A−D)
  • Recovering x* from the cg formula — always use cb* = (A−D) + (1−γ)x to find x*
  • Concluding risk-aversion alone is enough — for gamble acceptance, you still need u(·) to compare EU vs u(outside option)

Macroeconomics & Economic Growth
GDP · Production Function · Solow Decomposition · GDP per Capita · Circular Flow · COVID-19 · Dr. Rülke
What is Macroeconomics?
  • Micro vs Macro: Microeconomics studies individual decisions (consumers, firms, prices). Macroeconomics is micro-founded but analyses the aggregate economy.
  • Key macro questions: What drives growth? What causes inflation and unemployment? How should policy respond to shocks? What determines exchange rates?
  • Policy tools: Fiscal policy (G and T) and monetary policy (interest rates via central bank).
Economic models abstract from irrelevant details (ceteris paribus), show causal relationships, and are tested empirically. Endogenous variables are determined inside the model; exogenous ones come from outside.
GDP — Three Methods

Definition: Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given period.

  • Expenditure: GDP = C + I + G + (EX − IM). C = private consumption, I = real investment only (machines, buildings — NOT stocks/bonds), G = government spending, EX−IM = net exports.
  • Income: GDP = Σ(wages + profits + rents). Every euro produced is someone's income.
  • Value-Added: GDP = Σ(output − intermediate inputs). Counts only value added at each stage → equals the price of the final good.
Exam trap: Buying stocks or bonds does NOT count as Investment (I). Only physical/real investment creates productive capacity and counts in GDP.
  • Fundamental identity: GDP = Production = Income = Expenditure (holds in closed economy / long run).
  • Potential GDP = max output with all inputs fully employed. Output gap = Actual − Potential. Negative output gap = crisis/underperformance.
  • Long run: Determined by technology, labor, capital. Short-run crises do not determine long-run growth.
Cobb-Douglas Production Function

Formula: Y = T · Lα · K(1−α) where Y = output, T = technology (TFP), L = labor, K = capital, α = labor elasticity (0 < α < 1).

  • Growth decomposition (Solow): ΔY/Y = ΔT/T + α·(ΔL/L) + (1−α)·(ΔK/K)
  • Constant returns to scale: Doubling ALL inputs (L and K) exactly doubles output.
  • Diminishing returns: Adding more of ONE input (holding others fixed) gives progressively smaller gains.
  • Wage share insight: If labor market is efficient, wages/GDP ≈ α. Capital share ≈ 1−α.
Solow residual (USA 1850–1950): Technology = 52%, Labor = 38%, Capital = 10%. Technology is the dominant driver of long-run growth.
The Three Determinants of Long-Run GDP
  • Technology (T): Any increase in output WITHOUT adding more L or K. The "residual". Benefits rich (capital-intensive) economies more due to the concave production function — same tech shock yields larger absolute gains at higher K levels. "Technology drives a wedge between rich and poor countries."
  • Labor (L): More people → more total GDP, but GDP per capita typically falls (diminishing returns — each extra worker is less productive, output is divided among more people). Countries with high population growth tend to have lower GDP per capita.
  • Capital (K): Machines, buildings, infrastructure — anything that increases productive capacity. Capital is accumulated through investment (= savings in closed economy). Subject to diminishing returns.
Catch-Up Effect (Convergence Theory)
  • Theory: Poor countries have less capital → higher marginal product of capital → higher investment returns → FDI flows in → incomes converge toward rich countries.
  • Example: First road in a country = huge impact. 10th motorway in Germany = tiny marginal effect.
  • Reality — why it fails: Corruption, weak institutions, lack of legal security/property rights, political instability. Investors weigh risk: high nominal returns mean nothing if you can't recover your investment.
  • Result: No empirical evidence of convergence in most developing country samples.
GDP Per Capita — Four Determinants

Identity: GDP/Pop = (GDP/Hours) × (Hours/Employed) × (Employed/Labor Force) × (Labor Force/Pop)

  • Productivity (GDP/Hours) — output per hour = effective wage rate. Driven by education, tech, capital.
  • Hours worked (Hours/Employed) — avg hours per worker. USA works more hours than France → higher GDP/capita despite similar productivity.
  • Employment rate (Employed/Labor Force) — share of labor force with a job. Spain: high unemployment → low employment rate → lower GDP/capita.
  • Participation rate (Labor Force/Pop) — share of population in the labor force. Germany/Japan: aging population → falling participation rate → lower GDP/capita.
Participation rate ≠ Employment rate. Participation = who CAN work (labor force/population). Employment = who IS working (employed/labor force). Germany's main demographic challenge is a declining participation rate, not employment rate.
Circular Flow & Open Economy Identity
  • Basic circular flow: Firms pay wages/rents/profits to households. Households buy goods. Government collects taxes (T), spends (G). Households save (S) → Financial market → Investment (I).
  • Functions of money: (1) Medium of exchange, (2) Unit of account, (3) Store of value.
  • Open economy — Leakages (money OUT): Savings (S), Taxes (T), Imports (IM)
  • Injections (money IN): Investment (I), Government Spending (G), Exports (EX)
  • Equilibrium: S + T + IM = I + G + EX
  • Key identity: (S − I) + (T − G) = (EX − IM) → Private net savings + Budget balance = Current account balance
  • Twin deficit (Greece): Simultaneous current account deficit AND budget deficit → external debt/GDP >150% + public debt/GDP >150%. Caused by tax evasion, excessive spending, weak competitiveness.
  • Sustainable debt: GDP grows faster than debt → debt/GDP ratio shrinks over time → debt burden falls to zero.
⚠️ Common Exam Traps
  • Financial investment ≠ GDP investment. Buying stocks/bonds does NOT count as I. Only physical investment does.
  • Population growth ≠ GDP per capita growth. More people → more total GDP but LOWER GDP per capita (diminishing returns).
  • Participation rate ≠ employment rate — don't mix these up in the GDP/capita decomposition.
  • Tech shock benefits poor countries LESS — the concave production function means rich economies with more capital capture larger absolute gains from the same technology.
  • Catch-up theory sounds good but empirically fails — must explain WHY (bad governance, institutions, risk).
  • Imports = leakage, Exports = injection — not the other way around.
  • Sustainable debt = GDP growth rate > debt growth rate — about the differential, not the absolute level.
