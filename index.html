EU Law · Session V
Wrap-Up
BEPS & global tax reform, legal systems, rule of law, and EU history overview.
BEPS & Global Tax Reform

Triggered by public debate on legal tax avoidance by multinationals (GAFAs). OECD/G20 project with 148+ countries.

  • Pillar 1 — Reallocates taxing rights to market jurisdictions. Targets MNEs with >€20bn revenue & >10% profitability. Currently stalled — USA withdrew support under Trump.
  • Pillar 2 — 15% global minimum corporate tax for MNEs with >€750m revenue. Implemented in EU via Directive 2022/2523 (in force 31.12.2023). Key rules: IIR, UTPR, QDMTT.
  • Revenue lost to aggressive tax planning: USD 100–240bn globally (4–10% of global CIT). Developing countries suffer most.
  • EU implemented BEPS measures via ATAD (Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive).
Legal Systems & Rule of Law
  • Forms of legal systems: common law, code law, religious law, customary law.
  • Rule of law (Art. 2 TEU) — 6 aspects: legality, legal certainty, no arbitrary executive power, effective judicial protection, separation of powers, equality.
  • Council of Europe (≠ EU!) — 46 states, home of ECHR. Not to be confused with European Council or Council of the EU.
  • German Basic Law (GG) — 5 state principles: Democracy, Rechtsstaat, Republic, Federalism, Sozialstaat.
EU History — Key Timeline
  • 1951 Treaty of Paris → ECSC; 1957 Treaties of Rome → EEC + Euratom; 1965 Merger Treaty
  • 1979: First direct EP elections. 1985: Schengen. 1987: Single European Act.
  • 1992: Maastricht (monetary union). 1993: Copenhagen Criteria.
  • 2002: Euro. 2004: Big eastward enlargement. 2009: Lisbon (Charter binding). 2016: Brexit.
  • Charlemagne (747–814) — "Father of Europe": coronation 800 AD, Christianisation, administrative reforms.

EU Law · Session VI
Nature of EU Law
The 5 landmark cases shaping the EU legal order. Focus on the method, not just the facts.
The 5 Landmark Cases

The prof's emphasis: it's not about memorising every fact — it's about the method of working through a case.

Exam structure: (1) Identify EU vs. national law conflict → (2) Apply primacy → (3) Check direct effect → (4) Apply Simmenthal → (5) If directive not implemented → Francovich.
EU Legal Order Structure
  • Primary law: TEU + TFEU + Charter (equal legal value since Lisbon 2009)
  • Regulation (Art. 288(2)): binding in entirety, directly applicable — no MS implementation needed
  • Directive (Art. 288(3)): binding as to result — MS chooses form & method. Vertical direct effect possible once deadline lapses.
  • Decision (Art. 288(4)): binding on specific addressees only
  • Soft law: recommendations & opinions — not binding
1963
Van Gend en Loos · Case 26/62
⚡ Direct Effect
+ Show details
Facts: Dutch company's import duty raised 3%→8%, violating EEC standstill clause.

Holding: EEC = "new legal order." Individuals can directly rely on EU law without MS action.

3 Requirements: Provision must be (1) clear, (2) precise, (3) unconditional.

Basis: Art. 30 TFEU (ex Art. 12 EEC).
1964
Costa v. ENEL · Case 6/64
⚡ Primacy of EU Law
+ Show details
Facts: Italy nationalised electricity sector. Costa refused to pay Enel bill.

Holding: EU law prevails over all national law — including later national laws. MS permanently limited their sovereign rights. Without primacy, EU law would be meaningless.
1970
Internationale Handelsgesellschaft
⚡ Primacy over Constitutional Law
+ Show details
Facts: German court refused EU regulation as violating German constitutional fundamental rights.

Holding (double principle):
(1) EU law cannot be reviewed against national constitutions — only ECJ can declare EU law invalid.
(2) BUT fundamental rights exist as general principles of EU law.

Legacy: Led to the Charter of Fundamental Rights (binding since Lisbon 2009).
1978
Simmenthal · Case 106/77
⚡ Immediate Effectiveness
+ Show details
Facts: Italy charged Simmenthal border inspection fees incompatible with EU free movement rules.

Holding: National courts must immediately disapply conflicting national provisions — no waiting for constitutional court or parliamentary repeal.

Exam trap: Courts act immediately. No constitutional court needed.
1991
Francovich & Bonifaci · C-6/90, C-9/90
⚡ State Liability
+ Show details
Facts: Italy failed to implement Directive 80/987 by 1983 deadline. Employees lost wages with no remedy.

Holding: MS must compensate individuals for damage caused by failure to implement EU law.

3 Conditions: (1) Directive grants rights. (2) Content identifiable. (3) Causal link. + Deadline elapsed.

Note: Vertical direct effect only — no horizontal direct effect against private parties.
Art. 1
TEU — Foundation
EU founded on TEU + TFEU. Equal legal value.
The EU is founded on the TEU and TFEU, which have the same legal value. The EU replaces and succeeds the European Community.
Art. 2
TEU — Rule of Law
Founding values incl. rule of law & human rights.
6 aspects: legality, legal certainty, no arbitrary executive power, effective judicial protection, separation of powers, equality before the law.
Art. 5
TEU — Conferral
EU acts only within limits of conferred competences.
The EU has no inherent powers. It can only act where the Treaties explicitly grant competence. Powers not conferred remain with Member States.
Art. 6(1)
TEU — Charter Binding
Charter has same legal value as Treaties since Lisbon.
Since Lisbon (2009), the Charter of Fundamental Rights is legally binding primary law. Before Lisbon, fundamental rights existed only as general principles (Internationale Handelsgesellschaft).
Art. 19
TEU — Role of ECJ
Court ensures EU law observed. One judge per MS.
Establishes the CJEU. One judge per Member State. Judges must be independent. Court ensures uniform interpretation and application of EU law.
Art. 252
TFEU — Advocate General
AGs deliver independent, impartial opinions.
Advocates General deliver reasoned opinions — not binding but highly influential. Currently 11 AGs. Assist in achieving open, consistent application of EU law.
Art. 267
TFEU — Preliminary Ruling
National courts refer EU law questions to ECJ.
Primary mechanism for uniform application of EU law. Lower courts may refer; courts of last instance must refer. ECJ answers on (1) interpretation or (2) validity of EU law.
Art. 288
TFEU — Secondary Law
Defines regulations, directives, decisions, recommendations.
Regulation: Directly applicable — no MS implementation needed.
Directive: Binding as to result — MS chooses form & method.
Decision: Binding on specific addressees only.
Soft law: Not binding.
