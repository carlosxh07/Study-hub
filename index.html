EU Law · Sessions V & VI
Nature of EU Law
BEPS wrap-up, EU history, and the 5 landmark cases shaping EU legal order.
Session V — Wrap-Up

Session V recapped earlier material across three areas:

  • BEPS & Global Tax Reform — OECD/G20 project targeting multinational tax avoidance. Pillar 1: reallocates taxing rights to market jurisdictions (currently stalled — USA withdrew support). Pillar 2: 15% global minimum corporate tax for MNEs with >€750m revenue, implemented in EU via Directive 2022/2523.
  • Legal Systems & Rule of Law — Rule of law (Art. 2 TEU) has 6 aspects: legality, legal certainty, no arbitrary executive power, effective judicial protection, separation of powers, equality. Council of Europe (≠ EU!) — 46 states, home of the ECHR. German Basic Law: 5 state principles (Democracy, Rechtsstaat, Republic, Federalism, Sozialstaat).
  • EU History — 1951 ECSC → 1957 EEC/Euratom → 1992 Maastricht → 2002 Euro → 2009 Lisbon (Charter binding) → 2016 Brexit.
Session VI — The 5 Landmark Cases

The prof's emphasis: it's not about memorising every fact — it's about the method of working through a case. Always: identify the conflict → apply the principle → draw the consequence.

Exam structure tip: (1) Identify EU vs. national law conflict → (2) Apply primacy → (3) Check direct effect → (4) Apply Simmenthal → (5) If directive not implemented → Francovich.
EU Legal Order Structure
  • Primary law: TEU + TFEU + Charter of Fundamental Rights (all equal legal value since Lisbon)
  • Regulation (Art. 288(2)): binding in entirety, directly applicable, no national implementation needed
  • Directive (Art. 288(3)): binding as to result, MS chooses form & method. Can have vertical direct effect once deadline lapses.
  • Decision (Art. 288(4)): binding only on specific addressees
  • Soft law: recommendations & opinions — not binding
1963
Van Gend en Loos · Case 26/62
⚡ Direct Effect
+ Show details
Facts: Dutch company's import duty raised from 3% → 8%, violating EEC standstill clause.

Legal question: Can individuals directly rely on EU law in national courts?

Holding: Yes. EEC = "new legal order." Individuals have rights under EU law enforceable without MS action.

3 Requirements: Provision must be (1) clear, (2) precise, (3) unconditional.

Basis: Art. 30 TFEU (ex Art. 12 EEC).
1964
Costa v. ENEL · Case 6/64
⚡ Primacy of EU Law
+ Show details
Facts: Italy nationalised electricity sector. Costa refused to pay bill, arguing it violated EEC law.

Holding: EU law prevails over all national law — including later national laws. MS permanently limited their sovereign rights. Without primacy, EU law would be meaningless.

Key: "Special and original nature" — EU law is integral to national legal systems.
1970
Internationale Handelsgesellschaft
⚡ Primacy over Constitutional Law
+ Show details
Facts: German court refused EU regulation as violating German constitutional fundamental rights (Art. 2(1), Art. 14 GG).

Holding (double principle):
(1) EU law cannot be reviewed against national constitutions. Only ECJ can declare EU law invalid.
(2) BUT fundamental rights exist as general principles of EU law — EU institutions must respect them.

Legacy: Eventually led to the Charter of Fundamental Rights (binding since Lisbon 2009).
1978
Simmenthal · Case 106/77
⚡ Immediate Effectiveness
+ Show details
Facts: Italy charged Simmenthal border inspection fees incompatible with EU free movement rules.

Holding: National courts must immediately disapply conflicting national provisions — no waiting for constitutional court or parliamentary repeal. MS also cannot enact new laws conflicting with EU law.

Exam trap: Courts act immediately. No constitutional court needed.
1991
Francovich & Bonifaci · C-6/90, C-9/90
⚡ State Liability
+ Show details
Facts: Italy failed to implement Directive 80/987 (employee wage protection on insolvency) by its 1983 deadline. Employees lost wages with no remedy.

Holding: Member States must compensate individuals for damage caused by failure to implement EU law.

3 Conditions: (1) Directive grants rights to individuals. (2) Content of rights identifiable from the directive. (3) Causal link between breach and damage. + Deadline must have elapsed.

Note: Directives = vertical direct effect only. No horizontal direct effect against private parties.
Art. 1
TEU — Foundation
EU founded on TEU + TFEU. Equal legal value.
The EU is founded on the TEU and TFEU, which have the same legal value. The EU replaces and succeeds the European Community.
Art. 2
TEU — Rule of Law
Founding values incl. rule of law & human rights.
6 aspects of rule of law: legality, legal certainty, prohibition of arbitrary executive power, effective judicial protection, separation of powers, equality before the law.
Art. 5
TEU — Conferral
EU acts only within limits of conferred competences.
The EU has no inherent powers. It can only act where the Treaties explicitly grant competence. Powers not conferred remain with Member States.
Art. 6(1)
TEU — Charter Binding
Charter has same legal value as Treaties since Lisbon.
Since Lisbon (2009), the Charter of Fundamental Rights is legally binding primary law. Before Lisbon, fundamental rights existed only as general principles (Internationale Handelsgesellschaft).
Art. 19
TEU — Role of ECJ
Court ensures EU law observed. One judge per MS.
Establishes the CJEU (ECJ + General Court). One judge per Member State. Judges must be independent. Court ensures uniform interpretation and application of EU law.
Art. 252
TFEU — Advocate General
AGs deliver independent, impartial opinions to assist the Court.
Advocates General deliver reasoned opinions on cases — not binding but highly influential. Currently 11 AGs. Their role is to assist in achieving open, consistent application of EU law.
Art. 267
TFEU — Preliminary Ruling
National courts refer EU law questions to ECJ.
Primary mechanism for uniform application of EU law. Lower courts may refer; courts of last instance must refer. ECJ answers on (1) interpretation or (2) validity of EU law. Van Gend and Costa both came through this procedure.
Art. 288
TFEU — Secondary Law
Defines regulations, directives, decisions, recommendations.
Regulation: Binding, directly applicable — no MS implementation needed.
Directive: Binding as to result — MS chooses form & method.
Decision: Binding on specific addressees only.
Soft law: Recommendations & opinions — not binding.
