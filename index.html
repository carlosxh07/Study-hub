EU Law · Session I
German Tax System, DTCs & Digital Taxation
From §3 GFC tax definition through Germany–Luxembourg DTC analysis to BEPS, Pillar 2, and the digital tax gap.
A. What Is a Tax? — §3 German Fiscal Code (GFC)

A payment qualifies as a tax only if all five elements are present simultaneously:

  • Financial contribution — a payment in money
  • Imposed by law as a matter of public authority — not contractual
  • By a public-law body — state, Länder, or municipality
  • Without consideration — no direct benefit flows back to the payer
  • For the purpose of generating revenue — even as a secondary purpose

The "without consideration" element is the crucial legal distinction separating taxes from fees, contributions, and special levies.

⚠️ Exam trap: Fees are NOT taxes. A fee (Gebühr) follows the equivalence principle — you receive a specific public service in return (e.g. passport fee, court fee). This distinction determines constitutional basis and jurisdiction.
B. Germany's Tax System — Key Rates

Effective combined rate on corporate income from a German PE: CIT 15% + Trade Tax ~14% + Solidarity Surcharge ~1% ≈ 30%

  • Personal Income Tax (PIT): 0–45% progressive — individuals and partnerships (transparent entities)
  • Corporate Income Tax (CIT): flat 15% — capital companies (GmbH, AG)
  • Trade Tax (Gewerbesteuer): ~14%, levied by municipalities on trading profits
  • Solidarity Surcharge: ~5.5% on CIT/PIT liability — effectively ~1% of gross income
  • VAT: 0% / 7% (reduced: food, books) / 19% (standard)
  • Real Estate Transfer Tax: 3.5–6.5% depending on Bundesland
Entity type is decisive: rectangle = capital company → CIT | triangle = partnership → transparent/PIT | circle = sole trader → PIT | oval = permanent establishment

D. The DTC Analytical Method — "Solution Key" Exam Critical

Always follow this exact sequence for every DTC question:

  • Step 1 — State of Source (Germany): Establish Germany's domestic tax claim (~30% on PE profits)
  • Step 2 — Personal Scope: Art. 1 + Art. 3 (is the entity a "person"?) + Art. 4 (is it "resident" in the other state?)
  • Step 3 — Material Scope: Art. 2 — does the DTC cover this type of tax?
  • Step 4 — Income Type & General Rule: Art. 7 para. 1 — business profits taxed in state of residence only
  • Step 5 — PE Exception: Art. 7 para. 1 S1 HS2 — if a PE exists in Germany, Germany MAY tax
  • Step 6 — Auxiliary Activity Check: Art. 5 para. 4 — storage/delivery = auxiliary → NOT a PE → Germany cannot tax
⚠️ The ACo result: LuxCo's German warehouse is "auxiliary" under Art. 5 para. 4 → no treaty PE → Germany gets nothing. This is the Amazon/Google loophole in one rule.
E. The OECD, BEPS & Digital Tax Gap
  • OECD: 38 member states, Paris-based, advisory only — NOT binding, NOT an EU body. Produces the Model Tax Convention (MTC), which is the template for all bilateral DTCs.
  • Digital tax gap: No physical presence = no PE = no German taxing right. Spotify, Netflix, Google deliver services without ever being physically present → zero German income tax.
  • BEPS: Base Erosion & Profit Shifting — 15 OECD action points to ensure profits are taxed where economic activity occurs.
  • EU response: (1) Digital Services Tax — 3% on gross digital revenues, Art. 113 TFEU legal basis. (2) Deemed/digital PE — significant digital presence counts as a PE.
F. Pillar 2 — Global Minimum Tax

Applies to: MNE groups with global turnover ≥ €750 million AND effective tax rate (ETR) < 15%

ETR ≠ nominal rate. A company can have a 25% headline rate but a 5% ETR after deductions and structures. Pillar 2 targets the ETR.

  • IIR (Income Inclusion Rule): Top-down — the Ultimate Parent Entity (UPE) levies additional top-up tax on low-taxed subsidiaries. E.g. Hungarian sub pays 9% → German UPE charges extra 6%.
  • UTPR (Undertaxed Payment Rule): Bottom-up backstop — if IIR doesn't catch it, other group countries step in.
  • QDMTT: The low-tax country itself tops up to 15% first, before IIR applies.
Political fragility: Trump's election (Nov 2024) put the global deal "in peril" (FT). By Jan 2026, the US won exemptions. International tax cooperation depends entirely on political will.

EU Law · Session II
Legal Systems, Separation of Powers & the German Basic Law
From types of legal systems through the rule of law and democracy to the Enabling Act 1933 and the constitutional architecture of the Grundgesetz.
A. Types of Legal Systems

A legal system is a framework of rules, procedures, and institutions used to interpret, apply, and enforce laws. Its three features are: Laws/Codes, Institutions, and Procedures.

  • Common Law (England): Judges create binding precedent (stare decisis). Case law is central. Key countries: UK, USA, Australia, Canada, Ireland. Common law countries DO have statutes — the defining feature is binding precedent, NOT the absence of written rules.
  • Code Law / Civil Law (Roman Law): Comprehensive written codes define all law; judges apply codes, they do not create binding precedent. Key countries: Germany, France, Italy, Spain. Most EU members.
  • Religious Law: Legal system based on holy texts and religious traditions (e.g. Islamic Sharia law states).
  • Customary Law: Long-standing community practices that have acquired legal force. Generally unwritten (various tribal/indigenous systems).
EU relevance: The EU spans both traditions — Ireland (common law) vs. continental members (code law). EU legislation leans toward comprehensive written texts (code law influence) but CJEU case law also matters significantly (common law influence).
B. The Rule of Law — 6 EU Principles

The rule of law guarantees that all public powers act within constraints set by law, in accordance with democratic values and fundamental rights, and under independent courts. The EU's six principles (COM/2014/0158 final) — memorise all six:

  • (1) Legality — transparent, accountable, democratic, and pluralistic process for enacting laws.
  • (2) Legal certainty — laws are clear and predictable; citizens can foresee legal consequences.
  • (3) Prohibition of arbitrary exercise of executive power — the executive cannot act without legal constraint.
  • (4) Effective judicial protection by independent and impartial courts, including respect for fundamental rights and effective judicial review.
  • (5) Separation of powers — legislative, executive, and judicial branches are distinct.
  • (6) Equality before the law — the law applies equally to everyone.
⚠️ Exam trap: "Supremacy of EU law" is NOT one of the six rule of law principles — it is a separate EU constitutional law principle. World Bank data shows a strong positive correlation (0.73–0.77) between rule of law and economic prosperity.
C. Democracy & Separation of Powers

Democracy (from Greek demos = people + kratos = power) — rule by the people. Two forms: Representative democracy (citizens elect representatives — Germany's model under Art. 20(2) GG) and Direct democracy (citizens vote directly via referendums — Switzerland's prime example).

The separation of powers prevents dictatorship by ensuring no single branch accumulates too much power. Intellectual origins: John Locke (1632–1704) and Montesquieu (1689–1755).

  • Legislative: Enacts laws, sets policy, holds power of the purse — Bundestag + Bundesrat.
  • Executive: Implements laws, oversees day-to-day government — Bundesregierung.
  • Judicial: Interprets laws, resolves disputes, exercises judicial review — Bundesverfassungsgericht + federal courts.

Two dimensions: Horizontal (three branches checking each other) and Vertical (federal → Länder → local level). The "fourth estate" (independent press, coined by Thomas Carlyle 1841) is an unofficial watchdog on all three branches.

D. The Enabling Act of 1933 & Why the Basic Law Looks the Way It Does

Official name: "Act to Remedy the Distress of the People and the Reich" (Ermächtigungsgesetz), passed 24 March 1933.

  • Transferred legislative authority to Hitler's government — laws required neither Reichstag nor Reich President approval.
  • Laws could deviate from the Weimar Constitution — effectively suspending constitutional limits.
  • Vote: 444 to 94. Only the SPD (led by Otto Wels) voted against unanimously.
  • Extended in 1937, 1939, and 1943 — in force until 1945.

Lesson for the Basic Law: Democratic legitimacy can be hijacked through formally legal procedures. The Basic Law's response: (1) the Eternity Clause, (2) the Bundesverfassungsgericht as independent guardian, (3) fundamental rights placed FIRST.

E. The German Basic Law (Grundgesetz)

Adopted 8 May 1949 by the Parliamentary Council (representatives elected by the Länder parliaments). Entered into force 23 May 1949. Originally intended as temporary — now 75+ years old.

  • Fundamental rights placed first (Arts. 1–19) — signal that rights precede the state.
  • Five core state principles: democracy, rule of law, republic, federalism, social state.
  • Article 1 (human dignity) — the single most important article; every person has an inviolable right not to be treated as a mere object.
  • Eternity Clause (Art. 79(3)): Permanently protects the federal structure (Länder), Länder participation in legislation, and the principles of Arts. 1 and 20. Cannot be amended even by two-thirds majority.
  • Bundesverfassungsgericht (Karlsruhe) — "Guardian of the Basic Law," final say on constitutional interpretation.
  • Verfassungsbeschwerde — citizens can file constitutional complaints directly to the Federal Constitutional Court.
⚠️ Exam trap: The Eternity Clause does NOT prevent ALL constitutional amendments. Most articles CAN be changed with a two-thirds majority in both chambers — only the specific principles in Art. 79(3) are absolutely unamendable.
F. Fundamental Rights & the Democratic Legitimation Chain

Three categories of fundamental rights:

  • Status negativus — defensive rights protecting citizens from unlawful state interference (e.g. Art. 1(1) — human dignity; prohibition of torture).
  • Status positivus — rights to benefits; state must actively provide something (e.g. Art. 6(4) — mother's right to protection and care).
  • Status activus — participatory rights empowering citizens to shape the state (e.g. Art. 38 — right to vote; Art. 8 — freedom of assembly).

Fundamental rights primarily protect individuals against the STATE (public law), not against other private citizens (private law).

Democratic legitimation chain (Art. 20(2) GG): People → Election → Parliament → Legislation → Administration → Individual acts. Every state act must be traceable to the will of the people. Three requirements for any government action: (1) proportionality (suitable, necessary, appropriate), (2) legal basis / reservation of law (specific authorisation required), (3) legitimate purpose (public interest).

G. Council of Europe — Exam Trap Alert

The Council of Europe is completely separate from the EU. This is one of the most common student errors.

  • Council of Europe: Founded 1949 · 46 member states · Headquarters: Strasbourg · Purpose: human rights, democracy, rule of law · Key court: European Court of Human Rights (ECHR).
  • European Union: Founded (in current form) 1993 · 27 member states · Headquarters: Brussels/Luxembourg · Purpose: political and economic integration · Key court: Court of Justice of the EU (CJEU).
⚠️ Never confuse the ECHR (Council of Europe, Strasbourg) with the CJEU (EU, Luxembourg). Three completely different courts: Bundesverfassungsgericht (Germany), ECHR (Council of Europe), CJEU (EU).

Roman Law & the Foundations of Europe
From Rome 753 BC to Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis — the deep historical roots of every continental legal system.
A. The Four Phases of Roman History

Rome's legal development tracks its political evolution across four distinct phases:

  • Kingdom (753–510 BC): Founded 21 April 753 BC by Romulus (killed twin brother Remus). Law is unwritten, customary, and monopolised by the patrician elite — a tool of power, not justice.
  • Republic (510–31 BC): Senate and magistrates govern. 450 BC: the Law of the Twelve Tables breaks patrician monopoly. Law becomes a public, standardised system.
  • Imperial Period (31 BC–476 AD): Begins with Augustus. Julius Caesar (100–44 BC) preceded this era — his name is the etymological root of Kaiser (German) and Tsar (Russian).
  • Late Antiquity (~284–534 AD): Begins with the Diocletian Reform (284 AD). Culminates in Justinian's Corpus Juris Civilis (529–534 AD) — the legal capstone of Roman civilisation.
B. Law of the Twelve Tables (450 BC)

Plebeians demanded codification because patricians monopolised unwritten customary law to dominate court outcomes. Ten commissioners (decemviri) were appointed to write the law down and post it publicly in the Roman Forum — likely on bronze tablets.

  • Covered: civil procedure, property, family law, debt, and crime.
  • Core principle: Law must be based on written, public rules — not the private interpretation of the powerful. This is the conceptual root of the rule of law.
  • This is the moment law stops being a weapon of the elite and starts being a shared framework.
Exam relevance: Be able to explain in one paragraph why the Twelve Tables matter — written, public, standardised law as the birth of the rule of law.
C. The Byzantine Empire

When Theodosius I died in 395 AD, he divided the empire between his two sons: Arcadius (East → Byzantine Empire) and Honorius (West). The split was a practical response to the impossibility of governing the vast empire from one centre.

  • Western Empire fell 476 AD — Germanic leader Odoacer deposed Romulus Augustulus.
  • Eastern Empire (Byzantium) survived until 1453 AD — fall of Constantinople to the Ottomans.
  • Citizens called themselves Romans — "Byzantine" was coined by Western historians after the fall.
  • Key role: Byzantium is the transmission belt — it preserved Roman law, Greek philosophy, and Christian theology for ~1,000 years after Rome's collapse.
⚠️ Common trap: Do not confuse 395 AD (empire splits) with 476 AD (Western Empire falls) — these are two separate events 80 years apart.
D. Christianity & European Governance

Christianity's journey from persecuted sect to state religion is a case study in religion becoming a political tool. Key milestones:

  • 301 AD — Armenia: First Christian state in the world (not Rome!).
  • 311 AD — Edict of Toleration (Galerius): Ended empire-wide persecution of Christians.
  • 312 AD — Battle of Milvian Bridge: Constantine's Chi-Rho vision; beginning of Christian patronage.
  • 313 AD — Edict of Milan (Constantine & Licinius): Guaranteed full freedom to practise Christianity. Did not make it the state religion.
  • 321 AD — Sunday rest (Constantine): Historical origin of the work-free Sunday in the Western world.
  • 325 AD — Council of Nicaea: First ecumenical council; affirmed divinity of Christ; produced the Nicene Creed.
  • 337 AD — Georgia: Second Christian state (after Armenia, before Rome).
  • 380 AD — Cunctos Populos (Theodosius I): Christianity becomes official, binding state religion of Rome.
⚠️ Two traps: (1) Rome was NOT first — Armenia was (301 AD). (2) The Edict of Milan (313) ≠ Cunctos Populos (380). One guarantees freedom of worship; the other makes Christianity mandatory.
E. Corpus Juris Civilis (529–534 AD)

Commissioned by Emperor Justinian I of the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire. Purpose: to organise, streamline, and preserve centuries of accumulated Roman legal material. Contains four parts:

  • Codex (Code): Compilation of imperial enactments and statutes up to Justinian's reign.
  • Digesta / Pandects: Encyclopedia of extracts from Roman jurists' scholarly writings — most academically rich part.
  • Institutiones (Institutes): Student textbook introducing the Code — educational entry point.
  • Novellae (Novels): New laws enacted by Justinian himself after the Code.

Medieval reception: called ratio scripta ("written reason") — treated as the logical default standard to fill legal gaps. Rediscovered at Bologna (first European law school), spreading across Western Europe.

Modern legacy: Germany's BGB, France's Code Civil, Italy's Civil Code — all direct descendants. This is why continental Europe is a "civil law" jurisdiction.

⚠️ Major trap: The Corpus Juris Civilis was NOT a product of classical Rome. Compiled 529–534 AD by a Byzantine emperor — roughly 100 years after the Western Empire fell in 476 AD.
F. Rudolf von Jhering's "Three Times" Formulation Exam Quote

"Rome unified the world three times — through political power (the State), through religion (the Church), and through law (the reception of Roman law in the Middle Ages). The first was by force; the latter two by reason."

This explains why Roman law matters for EU law: the EU operates within civil law systems that all trace back to the Corpus Juris Civilis.

EU Law · Session IV
European History: Post-Rome to German Reunification
From the fall of the Western Roman Empire (476 AD) through Charlemagne, the Holy Roman Empire, Weimar, the Third Reich, Austria, and German reunification (1990). Focus: how each shift reshaped tax and legal systems.
A. After 476 AD — Germanic Successor Kingdoms

Odoacer deposes Romulus Augustulus in 476 AD → Western Roman Empire ends. Germanic successor kingdoms emerge: Franks (Gaul), Visigoths (Spain), Lombards (Italy). Roman law and Latin survived through the Christian Church. Byzantium (Eastern Empire) survived until 1453.

⚠️ Professor flagged: Until 25 November 2025, Latin was the only official language of the Roman Catholic Church — a direct thread of post-Roman continuity.
B. The Frankish Kingdom & Charlemagne
  • Clovis I founds the Frankish Kingdom in 481/482 AD (spanning modern France, Belgium, Germany)
  • Charlemagne crowned Emperor in Rome, 800 AD — revived the Roman imperial idea
  • "Father of Europe" — the EU's Charlemagne Prize (Karlspreis) is awarded annually in Aachen (his capital)
  • Treaty of Verdun (843 AD) divides the empire → eventually gives rise to France and the Holy Roman Empire

  • NOT a nation-state — loose confederation, no single capital → directly explains Germany's federal structure today
  • Main tax: excise duties (too fragmented for direct taxation)
  • Two dominant powers: Brandenburg-Prussia and Austria

Republic proclaimed 9 November 1918 — Germany's first parliamentary democracy.

Erzberger Reform (Finance Minister Matthias Erzberger, assassinated 26 August 1921):

  • Full centralisation of tax at Reich level (unitarism)
  • PIT: 10–60% | CIT: 10% retained profits, 20% distributed profits
  • Financial Equalisation Act (30 March 1920): Länder compensated with revenue sovereignty over property tax and trade tax
  • Church tax constitutionally guaranteed — still in force today (Art. 140 GG)
F. Hyperinflation & Its Legacy

Gold standard suspended 1914; WWI financed through war bonds → inflation built up. Hyperinflation peaked autumn 1923; stabilised by Rentenmark (15 November 1923). Savings wiped out → deep distrust of monetary instability in the German psyche.

⚠️ Direct legacy today: the constitutional debt brake (Schuldenbremse) since 2009. Professor stressed this is not just economic — it explains Germany's entire fiscal identity.
G. Third Reich — Enabling Act & Trade Tax

BEPS & Global Tax Reform

The BEPS (Base Erosion & Profit Shifting) project was triggered by public outrage over legal but aggressive tax avoidance by multinationals (especially GAFAs). Led by OECD/G20, involving 148+ countries.

  • Why it matters: USD 100–240bn lost globally to aggressive tax planning annually — 4–10% of global CIT revenue. Developing countries suffer most (high reliance on CIT, low enforcement capacity).
  • Pillar 1 — Reallocates taxing rights to market jurisdictions (where consumers are, not where HQ is). Targets MNEs with >€20bn revenue & >10% profitability. Currently stalled — USA withdrew support under Trump. Result: unilateral digital service taxes emerging globally (patchwork).
  • Pillar 2 — 15% global minimum corporate tax. Applies to MNE groups with >€750m annual revenue (~8,000 groups worldwide). Implemented in EU via Directive 2022/2523 (in force 31.12.2023).
  • Pillar 2 key rules: IIR (Income Inclusion Rule — top-up at parent level), UTPR (Undertaxed Payments Rule — backstop), QDMTT (domestic top-up tax — jurisdictions apply top-up to themselves first).
  • EU implementation: BEPS measures implemented via ATAD (Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive). Peer reviews monitor implementation of BEPS minimum standards.
  • Tax terminology distinctions: Tax saving (legal, no avoidance intent) → Tax planning (legal, structuring affairs) → Tax avoidance (legal but contrary to legislative intent) → Aggressive tax planning → Tax evasion (illegal).
Legal Systems & Rule of Law
  • Forms of legal systems: common law (case-based, UK/USA), code law (civil law, continental Europe), religious law, customary law.
  • Rule of law (Art. 2 TEU) — 6 aspects: (1) legality, (2) legal certainty, (3) prohibition of arbitrary executive power, (4) effective judicial protection, (5) separation of powers, (6) equality before the law.
  • Council of Europe (≠ EU!) — 46 member states, promotes rule of law, human rights, democracy. Home of European Court of Human Rights (ECHR). Russia excluded 2022. Not to be confused with: European Council, Council of the EU, or International Criminal Court (independent UN body).
  • German Basic Law (GG) — 5 state principles: Democracy (Art. 20(2) GG), Rechtsstaat, Republic, Federalism (Bund + 16 Länder), Sozialstaat. Safeguarded by Federal Constitutional Court (FCC) in Karlsruhe.
  • Separation of powers: Horizontal (legislative/executive/judicial — Montesquieu 1748, earlier: Locke 1689, Aristotle 4th c. BC) and Vertical (federal/Länder).
  • Democracy distinction: Popular sovereignty = source of power (people). Democracy = manner of exercising power. Direct vs indirect (referenda in Germany generally not possible — Art. 20 GG).
EU History — Key Timeline
  • 1945: WWII ends. UN founded (June 26). Churchill calls for "United States of Europe" (Sept 19, 1946) — but without UK.
  • 1949: Council of Europe founded (May 5).
  • 1950: Schuman Plan (May 9 — now Europe Day).
  • 1951: Treaty of Paris → ECSC (European Coal & Steel Community). 1957: Treaties of Rome → EEC + Euratom. 1965: Merger Treaty (institutions unified).
  • 1979: First direct EP elections. 1985: Schengen Agreement. 1987: Single European Act (extended competences, strengthened EP).
  • 1992: Maastricht Treaty (monetary union). 1993: Copenhagen Criteria (political, economic, acquis conditions for accession).
  • 2002: Euro introduced. 2004: Big eastward enlargement (10 CEE + Malta + Cyprus).
  • 2009: Lisbon Treaty → Charter of Fundamental Rights becomes legally binding.
  • 2016: Brexit vote. 2022: Russian aggression on Ukraine.
  • Charlemagne (747–814) — "Father of Europe": coronation 800 AD by Pope Leo III, Christianisation, administrative/educational reforms. Charlemagne Prize of Aachen awarded in his memory.

EU Law · Session VI
Nature of EU Law
The 5 landmark cases + interactive case analysis tool. Focus on the method.
The 5 Landmark Cases — Overview

These cases define how EU law relates to national law. The professor's focus: not memorising facts, but mastering the method — how to identify a legal issue and apply the right principle.

Exam method: (1) Identify conflict (EU vs. national law) → (2) Apply primacy (Costa) → (3) Check direct effect (Van Gend) → (4) National court duty (Simmenthal) → (5) Directive not implemented? → State liability (Francovich).
EU Legal Order — Full Structure
  • Primary law (Art. 1(3) TEU): TEU + TFEU + Charter of Fundamental Rights — all have equal legal value since Lisbon 2009. Art. 6(1) TEU makes Charter binding.
  • Regulation (Art. 288(2) TFEU): Binding in entirety, directly applicable in all MS — no national implementation needed. Example: passenger rights regulation.
  • Directive (Art. 288(3) TFEU): Binding as to the result to be achieved. MS chooses form and method. Can have vertical direct effect once implementation deadline lapses + provision is clear, precise, unconditional. No horizontal direct effect (individual vs. individual).
  • Decision (Art. 288(4) TFEU): Binding in entirety, but only on specific addressees (MS or individuals).
  • Recommendations & Opinions: Not binding — soft law. Used for guidance.
The 5 Cases — Quick Reference
  • Van Gend en Loos (1963) — Direct Effect. Individuals can rely on EU law directly. Requirements: clear, precise, unconditional. Art. 30 TFEU (ex Art. 12 EEC).
  • Costa v. ENEL (1964) — Primacy. EU law prevails over all national law including later legislation. MS permanently limited sovereignty. Without primacy, EU law would be meaningless.
  • Internationale Handelsgesellschaft (1970) — Primacy extends to constitutional law. National constitutions cannot override EU law. But: fundamental rights exist as general principles of EU law (pre-Charter).
  • Simmenthal (1978) — Immediate effectiveness. National courts must immediately disapply conflicting national law — no waiting for constitutional court or legislature. MS cannot enact new conflicting laws.
  • Francovich (1991) — State liability. MS must compensate individuals for damage from failure to implement EU law. 3 conditions: directive grants rights, content identifiable, causal link. + Deadline elapsed.
Direct Effect — Types & Limits
  • Vertical direct effect: individual relies on EU law against the state → allowed for both Treaty provisions and directives (once deadline lapses).
  • Horizontal direct effect: individual relies on EU law against another private party → allowed for Treaty provisions, but NOT for directives.
  • Why no horizontal DE for directives? Directives are addressed to MS — they only bind MS. Imposing obligations on individuals would bypass national implementation.
  • Remedy when no DE: Francovich state liability. Individual sues the MS for failing to implement, not the private party.
  • Indirect effect (consistent interpretation): National courts must interpret national law in light of the directive's purpose — even if direct effect not available.
1963
Van Gend en Loos · Case 26/62
⚡ Direct Effect
+ Show details
Facts: Dutch company (Van Gend & Loos) imported urea-formaldehyde from Germany. Dutch customs reclassified it under a higher-duty category, raising the duty from 3% to 8% — violating the EEC standstill clause on customs duties.

Legal question: Can individuals directly invoke EU law in national courts?

ECJ holding: Yes. The EEC constitutes a "new legal order of international law" — unlike ordinary international law, it creates rights for individuals which national courts must protect. The standstill provision (now Art. 30 TFEU) had direct effect.

3 Requirements for direct effect: Provision must be (1) clear, (2) precise, (3) unconditional — creating a right without further legislative action.

Significance: First time EU law was declared to create enforceable individual rights. Foundation of the entire EU legal order as we know it.
1964
Costa v. ENEL · Case 6/64
⚡ Primacy of EU Law
+ Show details
Facts: Italy nationalised its electricity sector, creating Enel. Costa (advised by Prof. Stendardi) refused to pay his Enel bill, arguing the nationalisation law violated EEC law. The Italian Constitutional Court had already ruled that the later Italian law should prevail over earlier EEC law.

ECJ holding: EU law is an integral part of national legal systems and takes precedence. MS permanently limited their sovereign rights by joining. A later national law cannot override earlier EU law.

Reasoning: If MS could override EU law by passing national legislation, the EU's functioning would be put in jeopardy. The EEC has a "special and original nature" that distinguishes it from general international law.

Consequence: National courts must apply EU law over conflicting national law — regardless of when the national law was passed.
1970
Internationale Handelsgesellschaft
⚡ Primacy over Constitutional Law
+ Show details
Facts: German private company faced forfeiture of its export licence deposit under an EU regulation. German court argued this violated German constitutional fundamental rights (Art. 2(1) — general freedom of action, Art. 14 GG — property rights).

ECJ holding (double principle):
(1) EU law cannot be reviewed against national constitutional law. The yardstick for EU law's validity is EU law only. Only the ECJ can declare EU law invalid — national courts cannot.
(2) BUT: fundamental rights form an integral part of the general principles of EU law, drawn from the constitutional traditions of MS and international instruments (esp. ECHR).

Historical context: At the time (1970), there was no written EU catalogue of fundamental rights. This case created the foundation for EU fundamental rights protection.

Legacy: Led eventually to the Charter of Fundamental Rights (2000, binding since Lisbon 2009).

Tension: German Federal Constitutional Court ("Solange" judgments) initially resisted this — still an ongoing constitutional dialogue.
1978
Simmenthal · Case 106/77
⚡ Immediate Effectiveness
+ Show details
Facts: Italian authorities required Simmenthal to pay fees for veterinary/public health inspections at the Italian border — incompatible with EU free movement of goods rules. Italy argued its national law (enacted under EC Treaty) would take precedence.

ECJ holding:
(1) Every national court must apply EU law in its entirety and protect rights conferred on individuals.
(2) Any conflicting national provision — even those enacted AFTER the EU rule — must be rendered inapplicable immediately.
(3) No need to wait for a constitutional court ruling or parliamentary repeal. National courts act immediately.
(4) MS also cannot enact new laws that conflict with EU law.

Why it matters: Operationalises primacy. Costa said EU law prevails in theory; Simmenthal tells courts exactly what to do about it in practice.

Exam trap: Courts do NOT need to refer to a constitutional court, legislative body, or the ECJ before disapplying national law.
1991
Francovich & Bonifaci · C-6/90, C-9/90
⚡ State Liability
+ Show details
Facts: Directive 80/987 required MS to create a guarantee fund protecting employees' wages if their employer went insolvent. Italy failed to implement this directive by the 1983 deadline. Francovich and Bonifaci were owed wages by insolvent employers and had no remedy because the fund didn't exist.

ECJ holding: MS are liable to compensate individuals for damage caused by their failure to implement EU law. This flows from the full effectiveness of EU law — if directives could be ignored without consequence, they would lose their binding force.

3 Conditions:
(1) The directive must grant rights to individuals.
(2) The content of those rights must be identifiable from the directive's text.
(3) There must be a causal link between the MS's breach and the individual's damage.
+ The implementation deadline must have elapsed.

Vertical DE vs Francovich: If a directive has direct effect → individual can rely on it against the state directly. If no direct effect (content not sufficiently clear, or horizontal situation) → Francovich state liability applies instead.

Why it matters: Without liability, MS would have no incentive to implement directives promptly. Completes the EU enforcement picture.

Individual Choice: Consumer Theory

Why Consumer Choice Matters

People choose the best option from what they can afford. Two ingredients: preferences (what is best?) and a budget constraint (what can you afford?). Neither matters without scarcity — a good is scarce if getting more of it requires giving something up. Business applications: optimal pricing, wage design, predicting consumer reactions to taxes.

Preferences & Bundles
  • Bundle: A combination (x₁, x₂) specifying amounts of two goods. Every point in a diagram is a bundle.
  • Notation: x ≻ y (strictly prefers x), x ~ y (indifferent), x ≽ y (weakly prefers x).
  • Three assumptions: (1) Monotonicity — more is better → ICs slope downward. (2) Transitivity — no cycles → ICs never cross. (3) Convexity — balanced bundles preferred → ICs bow inward.
  • Key insight: These assumptions are true often enough to be useful — they describe the vast majority of everyday economic decisions well.
Indifference Curves (ICs)
  • Definition: All bundles giving the same level of satisfaction. The consumer is indifferent between any two points on the same IC. Higher ICs = higher utility.
  • Contour map analogy: ICs are like altitude lines on a hiking map — moving to a higher IC means being better off.
  • Key properties: Downward sloping (monotonicity) · Never cross (transitivity) · Convex to origin (convexity).
  • IC formula (Karim): u(t,c) = (t−6)²(c−45) = ū → c = ū/(t−6)² + 45. Higher ū = higher, more preferred curve.
The MRS — Marginal Rate of Substitution
  • Definition: Units of good 2 willingly given up for one more unit of good 1, staying on the same IC. = Absolute value of IC slope at that point.
  • Formula: MRS = MU₁/MU₂. MU₁ (the good you get more of) always goes in the numerator.
  • Derivation: Along an IC, dU = 0 → MU₁·dx₁ + MU₂·dx₂ = 0 → dx₂/dx₁ = −MU₁/MU₂ → MRS = MU₁/MU₂.
  • Diminishing MRS: Moving right along an IC (more good 1), MRS falls. Scarce → precious (high MRS, steep IC). Abundant → less valuable (low MRS, flat IC).
Karim's MRS: u(t,c)=(t−6)²(c−45). MUₜ=2(t−6)(c−45), MU꜀=(t−6)². MRS=2(c−45)/(t−6). At A (t=15, c=540): MRS=110 (steep). At H (t=19, c=282): MRS≈36 (flatter). Diminishing MRS confirmed.
Utility Functions
  • Definition: u(x₁, x₂) assigns numbers such that preferred bundles get higher numbers: u(x) > u(y) ⟺ x ≻ y.
  • Ordinal, NOT cardinal: u=500 is better than u=200, but NOT "2.5 times better." Only the ranking matters, never the magnitude.
  • Existence: Guaranteed by the three preference assumptions together.
  • Marginal Utility: MU₁ = ∂u/∂x₁ and MU₂ = ∂u/∂x₂ — extra utility from one additional small unit, holding everything else constant.
The Budget Constraint & MRT
  • Budget constraint: p₁x₁ + p₂x₂ ≤ m. Budget set = all affordable bundles.
  • Budget line (BL): p₁x₁ + p₂x₂ = m → x₂ = m/p₂ − (p₁/p₂)x₁. Vertical intercept = m/p₂, horizontal = m/p₁, slope = −p₁/p₂.
  • MRT = p₁/p₂: Absolute slope of the BL — the rate at which the market requires you to exchange good 2 for good 1. Objective, market-determined.
  • Karim's BL: c = w(24−t) = 30(24−t). MRT = w = 30. Every extra hour of leisure costs €30 in forgone wages.
BL shifts: Income rises → parallel outward shift (slope unchanged). p₁ rises → BL pivots inward around x₂-intercept (vertical intercept fixed). p₂ rises → BL pivots inward around x₁-intercept (horizontal intercept fixed).
Optimal Choice: MRS = MRT
  • Goal: Maximise utility subject to the budget constraint. Monotonicity implies spending all income, so the problem is: which point on the BL gives the highest utility?
  • Graphically: The highest IC that still touches the BL — at the tangency point, the IC and BL have the same slope.
  • Condition: MRS = MRT. Consumer's personal willingness-to-trade = market's required rate.
  • If MRS > MRT: consumer values good 1 more than its market cost → buy more good 1 (work less for Karim).
  • If MRS < MRT: consumer values good 1 less than its cost → buy less good 1 (work more).
  • If MRS = MRT: no reallocation improves utility → optimal.
Two Solution Methods (always give the same answer)
  • Method 1 — MRS = MRT: (1) Write BL. (2) Compute MRS = MU₁/MU₂. (3) Set MRS = MRT. (4) Substitute BL into the condition. (5) Solve for x₁*. (6) Recover x₂* from BL.
  • Method 2 — Constrained Maximisation: (1) Solve BL for x₂(x₁). (2) Substitute into u(x₁,x₂). (3) Take the single-variable derivative w.r.t. x₁ (NOT a partial). (4) Set = 0. (5) Solve for x₁*, recover x₂*.
Karim (w=30): Method 1: MRS=MRT → 2(c−45)/(t−6)=30 → c−45=15(t−6). Sub BL: 30(24−t)−45=15(t−6) → t*=17, c*=€210. Works 7 hrs/day. Method 2 gives same: max (t−6)²(675−30t) → FOC → t*=17 ✓
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Flipping the MRS fraction — MU₁ (good on x-axis, the one you get more of) must go in the numerator. Flipping gives wrong optimal bundle.
  • Using partial derivatives in Method 2 — once the BL is substituted, take a regular single-variable derivative, not ∂u/∂x₁.
  • Treating utility as cardinal — u=1000 is NOT twice as good as u=500. Only ranking matters.
  • BL shift vs pivot confusion — p₁ rise = inward pivot (not parallel shift). m rise = parallel shift (not pivot).
  • MRT = w in labour-leisure model — the price of leisure is the forgone wage. Do not use abstract p₁/p₂.

The Core Problem

Every consumer faces two questions: What can I afford? (budget constraint) and What is best for me? (preferences / utility). The optimal choice is the bundle that maximises utility subject to the budget constraint — graphically, the highest IC that still touches the budget line.

Indifference Curves
  • Definition: All bundles (x₁, x₂) yielding the same utility level k. Set u(x₁,x₂)=k and solve for x₂ as a function of x₁.
  • Key properties: Higher curves = higher utility. Normally convex (prefer averages). Never cross.
  • Example: u = x_A·x_B = 100 → x_B = 100/x_A (hyperbola). At (5,20): slope = −100/25 = −4, so MRS = 4.
  • Weak preference set: All bundles weakly preferred to a given bundle — graphically, the area on and above the IC through that bundle.
Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)
  • Definition: Maximum amount of x₂ willingly given up for one more unit of x₁, keeping utility constant. = Absolute value of IC slope.
  • Formula: MRS = MU₁/MU₂ = (∂u/∂x₁)/(∂u/∂x₂)
  • Cobb-Douglas shortcut: For u = x₁ᵃ·x₂ᵇ: MRS = (a/b)·(x₂/x₁)
  • Why MRS changes: As you get more x₁ and less x₂, x₁ becomes relatively less valuable → MRS falls (diminishing MRS → convex ICs).
The Budget Constraint
  • Formula: p₁x₁ + p₂x₂ = m
  • Slope: −p₁/p₂ = MRT (market exchange rate)
  • Intercepts: x₁-axis: m/p₁. x₂-axis: m/p₂.
  • Tax on good 1: p₁ increases → x₁-intercept (m/p₁) falls → budget line rotates inward around the x₂-intercept (unchanged).
3-Step Solution Method (Standard / Cobb-Douglas)
  • Step 1: Calculate MRS = MU₁/MU₂
  • Step 2: Set MRS = p₁/p₂ → relationship between x₁ and x₂
  • Step 3: Insert into budget line → solve for x₁* and x₂*
  • Why it works: At the optimum, the consumer's subjective willingness to trade (MRS) equals the market's objective rate (p₁/p₂). If MRS > p₁/p₂, buy more x₁.
Budget share rule (Cobb-Douglas): x₁* = [a/(a+b)]·(m/p₁) and x₂* = [b/(a+b)]·(m/p₂). Budget shares are constant regardless of prices.
Special Preference Types
  • Perfect Substitutes u=ax₁+bx₂: MRS=a/b (constant). Compare with p₁/p₂: MRS>p₁/p₂ → buy only x₁; MRS<p₁/p₂ → buy only x₂; MRS=p₁/p₂ → any point on budget line.
  • Leontief u=min{ax₁,bx₂}: L-shaped ICs. Do NOT use MRS=MRT. Set ax₁=bx₂ (kink condition), insert into budget line. Why: no derivative at kink.
Two-Tariff Problems
  • Subtract fixed fee from income to get effective budget
  • Find optimal bundle under each plan using MRS=MRT
  • Compare utility levels → choose higher one
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Using MRS=MRT for Leontief preferences — impossible (no derivative at kink)
  • Forgetting to take absolute value of IC slope for MRS
  • Setting up wrong ratio: MRS = MU₁/MU₂, not MU₂/MU₁
  • For perfect substitutes with MRS=p₁/p₂: the entire budget line is optimal, not just one point
  • Confusing budget shifts (income changes) with rotations (price changes)

Risk and Lotteries
  • Lottery: A set of outcomes each with a probability. Σpᵢ=1.
  • EV = Σpᵢ·xᵢ — probability-weighted average of monetary outcomes. No utility involved.
  • EU = Σpᵢ·u(xᵢ) — probability-weighted average of utilities. What rational agents maximise. Apply u(·) first, then weight.
  • Critical distinction: EV and EU are different concepts. A risk-averse person values a lottery at less than its EV (CE < EV).
Risk Attitudes
  • Risk-averse: Concave u(·), u″<0, CE<EV. Example: u=√x.
  • Risk-neutral: Linear u(·), u″=0, CE=EV. Example: u=x.
  • Risk-loving: Convex u(·), u″>0, CE>EV. Example: u=x².
  • Why shape matters: Concave u → each extra euro gives less utility → losing €100 hurts more than gaining €100 helps → consumer dislikes risk.
CE, Risk Premium & Minimum Selling Price
  • CE: u(CE)=EU → solve for CE. Find: compute EU → invert u.
  • Risk Premium: RP=EV−CE. Positive for risk-averse. Maximum premium someone would pay to avoid risk.
  • Minimum selling price: p_min=CE. Set u(p)≥EU → solve. The seller only accepts a certain price ≥ CE.
  • Portia example: EU=1, CE=€1, EV=€4. She would sell for just €1 despite EV being €4.
Key exam trap: "He is risk-averse therefore he rejects the gamble" — WRONG without more information. Risk aversion tells us CE<EV, but not whether CE>0 (accept) or CE<0 (reject). Need exact u(x).
Insurance as a Consumer Choice Problem
  • Reframing: The two "goods" are consumption in the bad state (c_bad) and good state (c_good). Identical to PS1 — just different goods.
  • State-contingent incomes: c_bad = Endow_bad + (1−γ)·x ; c_good = Endow_good − γ·x (premium always paid).
  • Budget line slope: −γ/(1−γ) = MRT. To gain €1 in bad state, give up γ/(1−γ) in good state.
  • MRS: [p·u′(c_bad)] / [(1−p)·u′(c_good)]
  • Fair insurance (γ=p): MRS=MRT → c_bad*=c_good* → full insurance.
  • Unfair insurance (γ>p): Partial insurance only — c_bad*<c_good*.
6-Step Insurance Solution Method
  • 1) Write c_bad and c_good as functions of x
  • 2) Budget line: slope=−γ/(1−γ), use endowment point → intercept
  • 3) Write EU and compute MRS = [p·u′(c_bad)] / [(1−p)·u′(c_good)]
  • 4) Set MRS=MRT → relationship between c_bad* and c_good*
  • 5) Insert into budget line → solve for c_bad* and c_good*
  • 6) Recover x*: use c_bad*=Endow_bad+(1−γ)·x → x*=(c_bad*−Endow_bad)/(1−γ)
Sanity check: Verify MRS=MRT holds for your final c* values. Also: if γ=p, expect c_bad*=c_good*; if γ>p, expect c_bad*<c_good*.

  • Saying "he won't take the gamble because he's risk averse" — need the utility function (PS2.2 trap)
  • Forgetting to subtract premium from c_good — premium is always paid
  • Confusing EV and EU — using EV=4 instead of EU=1 for Portia's minimum price
  • Flipping MRS ratio: bad state always in numerator
  • Forgetting the (1−γ) factor when backing out x* from c_bad*

Why Risk Matters

Standard consumer theory assumes certainty. In reality — investing, insurance, employment — outcomes depend on circumstances outside your control. The key insight: the same tools from Ch2 (utility functions, budget lines, MRS = MRT) can handle uncertainty with just a reinterpretation.

Modelling Risk: States, Probabilities, Lotteries
  • States of the world: Each state s is a complete description of one scenario (e.g. "river dries up" vs "river flows"). With n states: s₁, s₂, …, sₙ.
  • Probabilities: State i occurs with probability pᵢ, where Σpᵢ = 1. These are objective probabilities.
  • Lottery: L = {p₁,x₁ ; p₂,x₂ ; … ; pₙ,xₙ} — the complete description of any risky situation. Every risky decision is a choice between lotteries.
  • Worked example (lecture): Win €90 (p=0.5) or €0 (p=0.5). u(0)=2, u(90)=12. EV = €45. EU = 0.5×12 + 0.5×2 = 7. Compare to u(45) to determine risk attitude.
Expected Utility Theory
  • Decision rule: EU(L) = Σᵢ pᵢ · u(xᵢ). The consumer chooses the lottery with highest EU.
  • Critical distinction — EV vs EU: EV = Σpᵢxᵢ (average money). EU = Σpᵢu(xᵢ) (average utility after applying u). They are equal only if u is linear.
  • Why u(·) matters: It captures how a person values money — specifically whether marginal utility is decreasing, constant, or increasing. This determines risk attitude entirely.
Risk Attitudes — The Key Chain of Implications
  • Risk-averse: Concave u(·) → decreasing MU → EU(L) < u(EV) → CE < EV → positive risk premium → buys insurance. Intuition: losing €50 hurts more than gaining €50 helps.
  • Risk-neutral: Linear u(·) → constant MU → EU(L) = u(EV) → CE = EV → zero risk premium → indifferent to insurance.
  • Risk-loving: Convex u(·) → increasing MU → EU(L) > u(EV) → CE > EV → negative risk premium → prefers more risk.
Practical significance: Risk aversion is the norm. It is why insurance markets exist — risk-averse people pay a premium to transfer risk to insurers who can pool it.
Certainty Equivalent & Risk Premium
  • Certainty Equivalent (CE): The guaranteed amount making the consumer indifferent to the lottery. Defined by u(CE) = EU(L). Find by: compute EU → invert u.
  • Risk Premium: RP = EV − CE. Maximum the person would pay to swap the lottery for its expected value with certainty. Quantifies how much risk matters.
  • Why it matters practically: RP = maximum insurance premium a person would willingly pay. Any premium below RP is individually rational to accept.
Insurance as State-Contingent Consumption
  • Reframing: Insurance moves consumption between states. The two "goods" are cg (good state consumption) and cb (bad state consumption).
  • Endowment (no insurance): cg = A, cb = A − D. Point (A−D, A) in the (cb, cg) diagram.
  • Coverage K at premium rate γ: cg(K) = A − γK; cb(K) = A − D + (1−γ)K.
  • Budget line slope: −γ/(1−γ) = MRT. To gain €1 in bad state, give up γ/(1−γ) in good state.
Optimal Insurance — MRS = MRT
  • MRS of EU ICs: MRS = [p · u′(cb)] / [(1−p) · u′(cg)]. Don't forget probability weights!
  • Optimality condition: p·u′(cb) / [(1−p)·u′(cg)] = γ/(1−γ)
  • Fair insurance (γ = p): Condition simplifies to u′(cb) = u′(cg) → cb = cg. Risk-averse consumer buys full insurance.
  • Unfair insurance (γ > p): u′(cb) > u′(cg) → cb < cg. Consumer buys partial insurance only. Full insurance line: 45° line where cb = cg.
  • Risk-neutral/loving under unfair insurance: Zero insurance (ICs too flat / concave to benefit).
Exam method: cg(x) and cb(x) → budget line → EU → MRS → MRS=MRT → solve for cb* → recover x* = [cb*−(A−D)]/(1−γ).
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Confusing EV and EU — always apply u(·) before weighting by probability
  • Forgetting probabilities in the MRS — MRS = p·u′(cb)/[(1−p)·u′(cg)], not just u′(cb)/u′(cg)
  • Using p instead of γ for the budget line slope — slope uses γ, not p
  • Assuming full insurance is always optimal — only true when γ = p (fair)
  • Swapping the endowment: bad state = A−D (not A), good state = A (not A−D)
  • Recovering x* from the cg formula — always use cb* = (A−D) + (1−γ)x to find x*
  • Concluding risk-aversion alone is enough — for gamble acceptance, you still need u(·) to compare EU vs u(outside option)

What is Macroeconomics?
  • Micro vs Macro: Microeconomics studies individual decisions (consumers, firms, prices). Macroeconomics is micro-founded but analyses the aggregate economy.
  • Key macro questions: What drives growth? What causes inflation and unemployment? How should policy respond to shocks? What determines exchange rates?
  • Policy tools: Fiscal policy (G and T) and monetary policy (interest rates via central bank).
Economic models abstract from irrelevant details (ceteris paribus), show causal relationships, and are tested empirically. Endogenous variables are determined inside the model; exogenous ones come from outside.
GDP — Three Methods

Definition: Total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given period.

  • Expenditure: GDP = C + I + G + (EX − IM). C = private consumption, I = real investment only (machines, buildings — NOT stocks/bonds), G = government spending, EX−IM = net exports.
  • Income: GDP = Σ(wages + profits + rents). Every euro produced is someone's income.
  • Value-Added: GDP = Σ(output − intermediate inputs). Counts only value added at each stage → equals the price of the final good.
Exam trap: Buying stocks or bonds does NOT count as Investment (I). Only physical/real investment creates productive capacity and counts in GDP.
  • Fundamental identity: GDP = Production = Income = Expenditure (holds in closed economy / long run).
  • Potential GDP = max output with all inputs fully employed. Output gap = Actual − Potential. Negative output gap = crisis/underperformance.
  • Long run: Determined by technology, labor, capital. Short-run crises do not determine long-run growth.
Cobb-Douglas Production Function

Formula: Y = T · Lα · K(1−α) where Y = output, T = technology (TFP), L = labor, K = capital, α = labor elasticity (0 < α < 1).

  • Growth decomposition (Solow): ΔY/Y = ΔT/T + α·(ΔL/L) + (1−α)·(ΔK/K)
  • Constant returns to scale: Doubling ALL inputs (L and K) exactly doubles output.
  • Diminishing returns: Adding more of ONE input (holding others fixed) gives progressively smaller gains.
  • Wage share insight: If labor market is efficient, wages/GDP ≈ α. Capital share ≈ 1−α.
Solow residual (USA 1850–1950): Technology = 52%, Labor = 38%, Capital = 10%. Technology is the dominant driver of long-run growth.
The Three Determinants of Long-Run GDP
  • Technology (T): Any increase in output WITHOUT adding more L or K. The "residual". Benefits rich (capital-intensive) economies more due to the concave production function — same tech shock yields larger absolute gains at higher K levels. "Technology drives a wedge between rich and poor countries."
  • Labor (L): More people → more total GDP, but GDP per capita typically falls (diminishing returns — each extra worker is less productive, output is divided among more people). Countries with high population growth tend to have lower GDP per capita.
  • Capital (K): Machines, buildings, infrastructure — anything that increases productive capacity. Capital is accumulated through investment (= savings in closed economy). Subject to diminishing returns.
Catch-Up Effect (Convergence Theory)
  • Theory: Poor countries have less capital → higher marginal product of capital → higher investment returns → FDI flows in → incomes converge toward rich countries.
  • Example: First road in a country = huge impact. 10th motorway in Germany = tiny marginal effect.
  • Reality — why it fails: Corruption, weak institutions, lack of legal security/property rights, political instability. Investors weigh risk: high nominal returns mean nothing if you can't recover your investment.
  • Result: No empirical evidence of convergence in most developing country samples.
GDP Per Capita — Four Determinants

Identity: GDP/Pop = (GDP/Hours) × (Hours/Employed) × (Employed/Labor Force) × (Labor Force/Pop)

  • Productivity (GDP/Hours) — output per hour = effective wage rate. Driven by education, tech, capital.
  • Hours worked (Hours/Employed) — avg hours per worker. USA works more hours than France → higher GDP/capita despite similar productivity.
  • Employment rate (Employed/Labor Force) — share of labor force with a job. Spain: high unemployment → low employment rate → lower GDP/capita.
  • Participation rate (Labor Force/Pop) — share of population in the labor force. Germany/Japan: aging population → falling participation rate → lower GDP/capita.
Participation rate ≠ Employment rate. Participation = who CAN work (labor force/population). Employment = who IS working (employed/labor force). Germany's main demographic challenge is a declining participation rate, not employment rate.
Circular Flow & Open Economy Identity
  • Basic circular flow: Firms pay wages/rents/profits to households. Households buy goods. Government collects taxes (T), spends (G). Households save (S) → Financial market → Investment (I).
  • Functions of money: (1) Medium of exchange, (2) Unit of account, (3) Store of value.
  • Open economy — Leakages (money OUT): Savings (S), Taxes (T), Imports (IM)
  • Injections (money IN): Investment (I), Government Spending (G), Exports (EX)
  • Equilibrium: S + T + IM = I + G + EX
  • Key identity: (S − I) + (T − G) = (EX − IM) → Private net savings + Budget balance = Current account balance
  • Twin deficit (Greece): Simultaneous current account deficit AND budget deficit → external debt/GDP >150% + public debt/GDP >150%. Caused by tax evasion, excessive spending, weak competitiveness.
  • Sustainable debt: GDP grows faster than debt → debt/GDP ratio shrinks over time → debt burden falls to zero.
⚠️ Common Exam Traps
  • Financial investment ≠ GDP investment. Buying stocks/bonds does NOT count as I. Only physical investment does.
  • Population growth ≠ GDP per capita growth. More people → more total GDP but LOWER GDP per capita (diminishing returns).
  • Participation rate ≠ employment rate — don't mix these up in the GDP/capita decomposition.
  • Tech shock benefits poor countries LESS — the concave production function means rich economies with more capital capture larger absolute gains from the same technology.
  • Catch-up theory sounds good but empirically fails — must explain WHY (bad governance, institutions, risk).
  • Imports = leakage, Exports = injection — not the other way around.
  • Sustainable debt = GDP growth rate > debt growth rate — about the differential, not the absolute level.

Business Cycle & Price Elasticity of Supply

The business cycle is a short-to-medium-term fluctuation (~5 years) of actual income around potential income. Sequence: Boom → Downswing → Recession → Trough → Upswing → Boom. Output gap = Actual income − Potential income. Positive = boom; negative = recession.

  • Price elasticity of supply: ηₛ = (dx/x)/(dp/p) = growth rate / inflation rate.
  • Elastic supply (ηₛ > 1): quantities respond more than prices → horizontal supply → demand shocks become real growth (recession scenario, spare capacity).
  • Inelastic supply (ηₛ < 1): prices respond more than quantities → vertical supply → demand shocks cause inflation, not growth (boom, full capacity).
  • Germany examples (1-month ηₛ): Gasoline 2.8, Milk 0.5, Electricity 0.2, Real Estate 0.1 — elasticity rises over time as firms can invest and expand.
Three Schools of Thought
Exam alert: The professor explicitly flags that exam questions frequently compare these three schools — especially their supply curve assumptions and policy stances.
  • Classical / Neoclassical (Smith, Ricardo, 1800–1933): Markets self-regulate ("invisible hand"). Long-run = always full employment. Vertical supply curve. No role for fiscal or monetary policy — complete crowding out.
  • Keynesian (Keynes, 1933): Sticky prices/wages → markets don't clear automatically. Unemployment can persist. Horizontal supply curve (spare capacity). Anti-cyclical fiscal policy is effective. Monetary policy ineffective (liquidity trap, investment trap).
  • Monetarism (Friedman, 1956): Government discretion causes cycles, not cures them. Private sector is stable. Rule-based monetary policy only. Natural unemployment rate exists.
Short-Run Goods Market Assumptions & Consumption Function
  • Short-run assumptions: Prices constant (P̄ = 1) · marginal costs fixed · unplanned inventories extremely costly · Y depends only on aggregate demand, not L, K, or T · closed economy (EX − IM = 0).
  • Consumption function: C = a + c·Y. a = autonomous consumption (minimum at Y=0; Germany: a = €50.2bn). c = MPC (0 < c < 1; Germany: c = 0.71 → €0.71 spent, €0.29 saved per extra euro).
  • Savings function: S = Y − C = −a + (1−c)·Y = −a + s·Y. MPS = s = 1−c. At Y=0: savings = −a (dissaving). Germany: S = −50.2 + 0.29·Y.
  • Disposable income: Y^v = Y − T + U. Consumption depends on Y^v, not gross Y.
Determinants of a (shift autonomous consumption): Expected income ↑ → C↑ · Wealth ↑ → C↑ · More equal distribution → C↑ · Expected inflation ↑ → C↑ · Interest rate ↑ → C↓ (saving more attractive).
Goods Market Equilibrium — 45° Diagram
  • Equilibrium condition: Ŷ = Y^S = Y^D. Requires stability condition 0 < c < 1.
  • 45° line: All points where Y^D = Y^S. C+I+G line has slope c < 1 → intersects 45° line at equilibrium point A = Ŷ.
  • Inflationary gap (Y left of Ŷ): Demand > Supply → unplanned inventories fall → firms produce more → Y rises toward Ŷ. Prices do NOT adjust.
  • Deflationary gap (Y right of Ŷ): Supply > Demand → inventories pile up → firms cut output → Y falls toward Ŷ. Prices do NOT adjust.
  • Alternative equilibrium: I = S (injections = leakages).
Worked example: C = 300 + 0.8Y, I^aut = 200 → Ŷ = 500/0.2 = 2,500 · Ĉ = 2,300 · Ŝ = 200 = I ✓
The Multiplier Effect
  • Multiplier: μ = 1/(1−c). With c = 0.8: μ = 5 → €100 of new investment raises GDP by €500.
  • Chain: ΔI = 100 → ΔY = 100 → ΔC = 80 → ΔY = 80 → ΔC = 64 → … Each round, (1−c) leaks into savings until convergence.
  • With proportional tax t: Effective multiplier = 1/(1−c+c·t) — smaller because taxes drain each round.
  • Multiplier types: G or I: 1/(1−c+c·t) · Transfer U: c/(1−c+c·t) · Tax T^aut: −c/(1−c+c·t) · Haavelmo: always = 1.
Fiscal Policy & Haavelmo Theorem
  • Budget balance: B = T − U − G. B > 0 = surplus. B < 0 = deficit.
  • Tax function: T = T^aut + t·Y. Proportional income tax t is a built-in stabiliser — automatically drains purchasing power in booms, reduces leakage in recessions.
  • Haavelmo Theorem: ΔG = ΔT → ΔY = ΔG. Balanced-budget multiplier = 1 always, regardless of c.
  • Why Haavelmo works: Government spends 100% (c^gov = 1). Households only consume fraction c of income loss. Net injection = (1−c)·ΔG. Through multiplier 1/(1−c) → total effect = ΔG.
Haavelmo algebra (c=0.8): ΔG=100 → ΔY_G = 100×5 = 500. ΔT=100 → ΔC = −80 → ΔY_T = −80×5 = −400. Net: 500−400 = 100 = ΔG ✓
Anti-Cyclical Fiscal Policy & Its Limits
  • Logic: Boom → restrictive (T↑, G↓, U↓ → surplus). Recession → expansionary (T↓, G↑, U↑ → deficit). Over a full cycle: budget balanced on average.
  • Six critiques (exam: state minimum 2):
  • ① Crowding out: G↑ → loanable funds demand↑ → i↑ → private I↓ → partially offsets stimulus.
  • ② Wage inflation: Expansionary spending triggers wage claims → higher costs → inflation.
  • ③ Asymmetric behaviour: Politicians expand in recessions but fail to cut in booms → rising structural debt.
  • ④ Budget rigidity: Not all spending lines can be changed quickly.
  • ⑤ Political incentives: Governments offer electoral spending for popularity, not economic need.
  • ⑥ Time lags: Inside lag (information + decision) + Outside lag (action + policy + effectiveness).
IS Curve & Investment Function
  • Investment function: I = I^aut + b·Y − g·i. Simplified (b=0): I = I^aut − g·i.
  • IS curve: All (Y, i) pairs where goods market is in equilibrium (I = S). Slopes downward: lower i → I↑ → Y↑.
  • Shifts right: G↑, T↓, U↑, a↑, I^aut↑ (any expansionary shock).
  • Shifts left: G↓, T↑, a↓ (saving paradox), I^aut↓.
  • Vertical IS (g = 0): Investment completely insensitive to interest rate → monetary policy has no effect on goods market (investment trap).
  • Saving paradox: If all households save more (a↓): C↓ → Y↓ → s·Y↓ → total savings actually fall. Individually rational, collectively self-defeating.
IS derivation (2025 exam): C=250+0.5Y^v, I=265−7i, G=200, T=100+0.1Y → Y^v=0.9Y−100 → C=200+0.45Y → 0.55Y=665−7i → IS: Y = 1,209.09 − 12.73i
Investment Function & S = I Equilibrium ⭐ Exam-flagged

Goods market equilibrium can always be written as S = I (leakages = injections). Understanding what happens when they diverge — and why the economy converges back — is a core exam skill.

  • Full investment function: I = I^aut + b·Y − g·i
    • I^aut — autonomous investment: firms' baseline decisions, independent of current income or interest rates.
    • +b·Y — income-induced investment (b > 0): higher expected output → firms invest more. Example: a retailer expecting higher consumer incomes next year orders more inventory and equipment today.
    • −g·i — interest-rate-induced investment (g > 0): lower borrowing cost → more projects become profitable. Example: at 10% interest, a factory expansion earns a negative return; at 2%, the same project is highly profitable.
    • Simplified for IS derivation (b = 0): I = I^aut − g·i
  • When I > S: More is injected than leaks out → income and output rise → Y↑ → S rises until S = I again. Real example: post-war West German reconstruction — massive capital investment drove rising wages and eventually rising savings until a new higher equilibrium was reached.
  • When S > I: More leaks out than is injected → demand falls → Y falls → Keynesian recession. Excess saving destroys income rather than financing growth.
  • Interest rate elasticity of investment g: Measures how strongly I responds to a 1pp change in i. Large g → flat IS curve (monetary policy potent). g = 0 → vertical IS (monetary policy impotent).
Formula interpretation — I = 200 − 5i: At i = 5%: I = 175. At i = 2%: I = 190. Every 1pp fall in the interest rate raises investment by 5 units (g = 5). This is moderate interest-elasticity — the IS curve would have a meaningful negative slope.
Saving Paradox — Mechanism & Japan Example ⭐ Exam-flagged

The professor explicitly called this a key exam concept and required Japan as the empirical example.

  • The paradox: What is rational for one household (save more to build security) becomes collectively self-defeating when all households do it simultaneously.
  • Exact chain (as on the slides): −a↑ → a↓ → C↓ → Y↓ → s·Y↓ → total S falls = −a↑ + s·Y↓. The income effect (falling Y reduces the s·Y term) dominates the direct saving intent.
  • Graphically: The S-curve shifts upward (−a becomes a larger negative number), but the new equilibrium Ŷ is lower, and at that lower Y total savings (−a + s·Ŷ) can actually be lower than before — the paradox is complete.
  • Japan since the 1990s (required example): Aging population → rising precautionary saving → weaker consumption → firms invest less → GDP stagnated for two decades. Japanese household savings actually fell by roughly 30% over this period as incomes declined — exactly the paradox mechanism.
  • Policy implication: In a Keynesian recession, collective saving makes things worse. The case for fiscal stimulus rests partly on this: the government must inject demand to break the deflationary saving spiral.
Exam answer template: State the paradox → write the chain (−a↑ → a↓ → C↓ → Y↓ → s·Y↓ → S↓) → give Japan as the empirical case → conclude: individually rational, collectively self-defeating.
Economic Terminology — Precise Definitions ⭐ Exam-flagged

These exact definitions appear on the slides. Recession is definitely exam-relevant; the others help distinguish situations with different policy implications.

  • Recession: Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth. Limited to a country or region. Part of the normal business cycle. Example: Germany Q3–Q4 2023.
  • Depression: Severe and prolonged downturn, typically exceeding −10% growth. Not part of the regular business cycle. Has global impact. Example: Great Depression 1929–1933 — US GDP fell ~30%, global unemployment exceeded 25%.
  • Stagnation: Slowdown of long-term economic growth due to structural changes (not a cyclical dip). Example: Japan's two lost decades from 1991 onward — prolonged near-zero growth despite no acute recession.
  • Stagflation: Inflation >5% AND negative growth rate simultaneously. The worst of both worlds — standard stabilisation tools conflict (stimulus fights unemployment but worsens inflation). Example: Oil-price shocks of the 1970s hit Western economies with both recessions and double-digit inflation.
  • Hyperinflation: Inflation >50% per month. Money ceases to function as a store of value. Example: Germany 1923 (Weimar Republic), Zimbabwe 2008.
Key distinction: Recession = cyclical + regional. Depression = structural + global. Stagflation is special: anti-cyclical policy becomes self-contradictory — cutting taxes to fight unemployment simultaneously fuels inflation.
Time Lags in Fiscal Policy — Full 5-Stage Model ⭐ Exam-flagged

The professor called this one of the biggest practical weaknesses of anti-cyclical fiscal policy and flagged it for the exam. The slides build the timeline step by step.

  • ① Information lag: Recognising the recession. GDP is measured quarterly and takes weeks to compile — two consecutive negative quarters must be observed before a recession is even officially declared. Months already lost.
  • ② Decision lag: Political debate, committee hearings, and budget negotiations. In Germany: ~6–9 months. Angela Merkel famously joked she dreaded calls from the Federal Statistics Office (area code 0611, Destatis) — highlighting how late statistical data arrives to policymakers.
  • ③ Action lag: Implementing the decision — awarding contracts, distributing grants, starting projects. Germany's Corona relief (€100bn approved by Altmaier) took until late January 2021 to actually pay out to businesses in crisis.
  • ④ Policy lag: The spending begins affecting aggregate demand, but each round of the multiplier takes time to work through the economy.
  • ⑤ Effectiveness lag: The final rounds of the multiplier complete — GDP fully reflects the intervention.
Inside lag = ① + ② + ③  |  Outside lag = ④ + ⑤

Critical implication: Total delay often exceeds one year. The economy may have already recovered by the time stimulus arrives — turning anti-cyclical policy pro-cyclical by accident (stimulus hits during a boom, adding inflationary pressure). Haavelmo himself noted this: balanced-budget fiscal policy is more predictable precisely because it bypasses the lag problem.

Open Economy: Leakages & Injections

Extending the S = I framework to an open economy with government: equilibrium requires all leakages to equal all injections.

  • Leakages (money leaving the income-spending circuit):
    • Savings (S) — households withhold income from consumption
    • Taxes (T) — government withdraws income from households and firms
    • Imports (IM) — spending flows to foreign producers, not domestic ones
  • Injections (money entering the circuit):
    • Investment (I) — firms add to domestic productive capacity
    • Government spending (G) — direct demand injection into domestic market
    • Exports (EX) — foreign demand for domestically produced goods
  • Equilibrium identity: S + T + IM = I + G + EX. Rearranging: (S − I) + (T − G) = (EX − IM). Private surplus + Government surplus = Trade surplus.
Germany example: Germany's large trade surplus (EX > IM) must be "funded" by either households saving more than firms invest (S > I) or the government running a surplus (T > G) — or some combination. This identity holds by national accounting and cannot be violated.
⚠️ Most Common Mistakes
  • Wrong multiplier with taxes: Use 1/(1−c+c·t), NOT 1/(1−c) when there is a proportional income tax.
  • Haavelmo vs. normal multiplier: Haavelmo = 1 always. Never apply 1/(1−c) to a balanced-budget change.
  • Transfer vs. spending multiplier: Transfer (ΔU) is smaller by factor c — must pass through household consumption first.
  • Gap direction: Inflationary gap = LEFT of Ŷ (demand > supply, inventories shrink). Deflationary = RIGHT (supply > demand, inventories pile up).
  • Savings function intercept sign: S = −a + s·Y. Intercept is NEGATIVE (−a). At Y=0: dissaving.
  • What adjusts in short run: Only quantities (output, inventories). Prices do NOT adjust. Never say "prices fall in a deflationary gap."
  • Tax multiplier sign: ΔT^aut raises taxes → multiplier is NEGATIVE. A tax CUT stimulates (ΔT negative → ΔY positive).
  • IS curve shifts vs. movements: A change in i moves you along the IS curve. A change in G, T, a, or I^aut shifts the curve. Never say "monetary policy shifts the IS curve."
  • Saving paradox direction: More saving → lower Y → lower total savings. The chain goes: −a↑ → C↓ → Y↓ → s·Y↓ → S↓. Savings fall, not rise, in the end.
  • Depression vs. recession: A depression is >−10%, global, not part of the business cycle. A recession is two negative quarters, regional, cyclical. Never conflate them.
